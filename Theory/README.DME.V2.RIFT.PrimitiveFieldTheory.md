# RIFT T0 — Primitive Field Theory

## Purpose

Primitive Field Theory defines the minimal conceptual substrate required for DME/RIFT to model:

- distinguishable structure
- lawful transition
- admissibility
- relation
- recursion
- proof
- authority
- governed consequence

It does not define:

- semantics
- truth
- policy
- agents
- implementation objects
- governance procedure
- runtime schema

It defines the field conditions under which later structures may emerge, be measured, be tested, and be governed.

---

## Core Thesis

A field is a bounded space of possible states under constraint.

A structure becomes observable when differences within that field become distinguishable through a bounded observer, lens, locality, attention, uncertainty boundary, and admissible transition rule.

Cognition, in this framework, is recursive distinction stabilization:

> distinguishable structure persists through energetic, informational, relational, and admissibility-constrained feedback until it becomes eligible for identity proof and governed consequence.

---

# 1. Primitive Definitions

---

## Primitive 1 — Field

A Field is a bounded space of possible states under constraint.

```text
F = { S | Θ }
```
Where:

F = field
S = possible states
Θ = constraints defining valid state space

A field may be physical, symbolic, computational, relational, energetic, probabilistic, or abstract.

No field exists without constraint.

## Primitive 2 — State

A State is a configuration within a field.

s ∈ F

A state is not identity.

A state becomes relevant only when it participates in distinguishable change, relation, persistence, proof, or consequence.

## Primitive 3 — Potential

Potential is the available possibility for state transition.

Π(s → s')

Potential includes:

possible transitions
unresolved option space
activation likelihood
available energy
probability distributions
latent structure

Potential is broader than probability.

Probability is one measurable expression of potential.

## Primitive 4 — Constraint

A Constraint limits possible states or transitions.

Θ : S → admissible / inadmissible

Constraints define:

boundaries
valid transformations
possible trajectories
admissible interactions
scope limits
authority ceilings

Constraint is the condition that makes field behavior legible.

## Primitive 5 — Energy

Energy is the capacity for structure to change, propagate, interact, or maintain persistence.

Energy enables:

activation
attention
transformation
interference
recursion
persistence maintenance

Energy is not authority.

Energy may affect runtime without validating identity.

## Primitive 6 — Information

Information is distinguishable structure relative to an observer, lens, context, and constraint.

Information = distinguishable structure | observer, lens, locality, constraint

Raw data is not yet information.

Raw data becomes information when it can be distinguished under a valid observation frame.

## Primitive 7 — Uncertainty

Uncertainty is unresolved distinguishability.

U(s | O, L, Θ) = unresolved distinguishability

Uncertainty may arise from:

insufficient resolution
cross-basis disagreement
missing axes
unstable state evolution
ambiguous boundaries
lens bias
incomplete provenance

Uncertainty is not error by default.

Uncertainty must be preserved until resolved, bounded, deferred, or routed.

## Primitive 8 — Locality

Locality is the bounded region within which observation, attention, influence, and transition may occur.

Loc(O, F, Θ) = reachable / observable / influence-admissible region

Locality defines:

observer boundary
reachable states
valid neighbors
interaction range
field resolution domain
propagation path

No observation is global by default.

## Primitive 9 — Observer

An Observer is a bounded system that can form distinctions through attention.

Observer =
  boundary
+ state
+ constraint
+ lens
+ attention
+ memory/trace
+ locality

An observer may be biological, computational, instrumental, runtime-local, or projection-bound.

An observer does not define absolute reality.

An observer defines a local domain of distinguishability.

## Primitive 10 — Lens

A Lens is a declared basis for observation.

Lens = basis + scale + metric + domain + uncertainty + bias

Every lens introduces bias.

Bias is not error by default.

Bias is the structural effect of observation.

## Primitive 11 — Attention

Attention is bounded energy allocation over distinguishability.

Att(O, F, L, Θ, B) → W

Where:

O = observer
F = field
L = lens
Θ = constraints
B = available budget
W = attention weighting over distinguishable regions

Attention determines:

what is sampled
what is amplified
what uncertainty is processed
what interactions consume budget
which local frame becomes operationally relevant

Attention is not authority.

## Primitive 12 — Difference

Difference is variation between states.

Δ(sᵢ, sⱼ)

Difference is not yet distinction.

Difference becomes distinction only when it is detectable, bounded, supported, admissible, provenance-linked, and uncertainty-declared.

## Primitive 13 — Distinction

A Distinction is a bounded, observer-relative difference that satisfies support, admissibility, provenance, and uncertainty conditions.

Distinction =
  resolvable difference
| observer
| lens
| locality
| constraint
| support
| uncertainty
| lineage

A distinction is not:

raw feature
semantic label
identity
proof
authority

A distinction is the first runtime-selectable unit of cognition.

Distinction Geometry implements this as a multi-chart, multi-scale field of distinguishable structure derived from cross-basis analysis rather than semantic event projection.

## Primitive 14 — Transition

A Transition is a movement from one state/configuration to another under a lawful operator path.

T : s → s'

A transition may be:

structural
runtime
relational
behavioral
proof-directed
projection-bound
governance-relevant

A transition is not automatically admissible.

A transition is not automatically consequential.

Transitions are runtime paths.

Governance frames are consequence envelopes around proposed transition effects.

## Primitive 15 — Admissibility

Admissibility is the local field of permitted transition.

Admissible(s → s') iff
  transition remains inside declared constraint envelope

Admissibility governs:

activation
interaction
transition
branching
projection routing
proof entry
deferral
quarantine
candidate emission

Admissibility does not govern consequence.

Admissibility is not authority.

The Admissibility Field operationalizes runtime permission through axes such as locality validity, lineage validity, uncertainty bounds, budget/cost bounds, salience threshold, distinguishability support, constraint compatibility, temporal validity, projection boundary, and proof precondition.

## Primitive 16 — Relation

A Relation is a constraint-mediated dependency between distinctions whose interaction produces measurable, repeatable state change.

A relation is not similarity alone.

A relation is not proof.

A relation is stabilized dependency.

The Relation Operator formalizes relation as stabilized interference with declared dependency type, not raw similarity or semantic association.

## Primitive 17 — Structure

Structure is a set of distinctions, relations, and constraints stabilized enough to be tracked.

Structure = { distinctions + relations + constraints }

Structure is not identity until it survives transformation, perturbation, or reconstruction challenge.

## Primitive 18 — Invariance

Invariance is what remains conserved under transformation, context shift, perturbation, or replay.

Invariance is the basis of identity proof.

The invariant identity space uses six comparison axes:

I(x) = [
  composition,
  magnitude,
  alignment,
  persistence,
  separation,
  convergence
]

These are comparison axes, not semantic meanings. The invariant metric defines similarity as relational evidence, not proof.

## Primitive 19 — Identity

Identity is structure that preserves invariance across declared transformations, perturbations, context shifts, and reconstruction conditions.

Identity is not:

label
meaning
truth
salience
persistence alone
authority

Identity is scoped survival through change.

T4 tests identity through proof ecology: proof is survival under lawful perturbation while preserving invariance, uncertainty, lineage, and admissibility bounds.

## Primitive 20 — Proof

Proof is constrained survival testing.

Proof =
  candidate survival
  under declared perturbation
  within scope
  preserving invariance, lineage, uncertainty, and admissibility

Proof does not authorize consequence.

Proof produces evidence.

Proof Execution operationalizes this as candidate formation, admissibility gating, perturbation execution, proof traces, proof objects, proof failure objects, and routing outcomes.

## Primitive 21 — Projection

Projection is a bounded view of system state under declared lens, scope, loss, uncertainty, and authority ceiling.

Projection is not the thing itself.

Projection may expose:

payload views
distinction slices
relation views
proof summaries
reconstruction views
decision topology views
agent-readable interfaces

Projection cannot exceed its declared authority ceiling.

The Projection / Index Substrate defines projection as bounded access, not truth.

## Primitive 22 — Retention

Retention is the governed persistence of trace, structure, proof, failure, projection, or authority state across time.

Retention may support:

Echo
reconstruction
recurrence
proof depth
causality signatures
governance audit
memory continuity

Retention is not identity by itself.

Retention is not authority by itself.

Retention must declare duration, decay, echo eligibility, lineage, uncertainty, and authority ceiling.

## Primitive 23 — Agency

Agency is projection-bound capacity to select, propose, simulate, request, or act under scoped authority and admissibility constraints.

Agency is not default autonomy.

Agency is not identity.

Agency is not authority by itself.

An agent may operate only through declared:

projection scope
admissibility profile
proposal rights
simulation rights
tool scope
proof request rights
governance request rights
revocation conditions

T5 defines agents as projection-bound and profile-bound actors that cannot self-promote authority or mutate the ledger/governance layer directly.

## Primitive 24 — Authority

Authority is the permitted consequence of a structure under declared scope.

Authority(x) =
  allowed_effects(
    x |
    proof,
    scope,
    lineage,
    uncertainty,
    admissibility_profile,
    governance_decision,
    context
  )

Authority is not:

truth
proof
salience
recurrence
persistence
projection
admissibility
confidence

Authority is a bounded, revocable constraint field over permitted consequence.

T5 defines authority as vectorial across epistemic, operational, mutation, exposure, retention, agent, and admissibility-configuration dimensions.

## Primitive 25 — Governance

Governance is the procedure that evaluates, grants, limits, configures, revokes, records, and audits authority and admissibility effects.

Governance answers:

What may this surviving or proposed structure change?

Governance is not truth.

Governance is consequence control.

Governance preserves the boundary:

active ≠ admissible
admissible ≠ proven
proven ≠ authorized
authorized ≠ permanent

## Primitive 26 — Recursion

Recursion is feedback over structure across time, scale, representation, relation, proof, or authority.

R(s_t) = f(s_t, trace_{t-n:t}, Θ)

Recursion may occur as:

structural recursion
temporal recursion
attentional recursion
relational recursion
proof recursion
projection recursion
authority recursion
admissibility recursion

Recursion requires trace.

Recursion is not automatically intelligence.

Cognition requires recursion that stabilizes distinctions into identity through constrained feedback.

# 10. Epistemic Boundary

RIFT does not require metaphysical claims about consciousness creating matter.

Any observer or measuring system perturbs what it can distinguish, because observation requires interaction, energy allocation, constraint, locality, and feedback.

Thus DME treats attention as operationally causal within the runtime field while leaving broader metaphysical claims outside T0 authority.

# 11. Core Laws
Law 1 — No Field Without Constraint

A field is only meaningful when possible states are bounded.

Law 2 — No Observation Without Locality

Every observation occurs within a bounded domain of possible distinction.

Law 3 — No Attention Without Budget

Attention is finite and must allocate bounded energy.

Law 4 — No Distinction Without Uncertainty Boundary

Every distinction must declare what remains unresolved.

Law 5 — No Transition Without Admissibility

A state change must remain inside declared runtime permission geometry.

Law 6 — No Relation Without Stable Dependency

A relation requires repeatable, measurable interaction, not similarity alone.

Law 7 — No Identity Without Invariance

Identity requires survival through declared transformation, perturbation, or reconstruction challenge.

Law 8 — No Proof Without Scope

Proof must declare candidate, lineage, basis, context, perturbation bounds, uncertainty, and declared use.

Law 9 — No Authority Without Consequence Boundary

Authority must declare what it permits, what it forbids, where it applies, and when it expires or may be revoked.

Law 10 — No Recursion Without Trace

Recursive feedback requires retained structure, memory, lineage, or reconstructable continuity.

Law 11 — No Projection As Source

A projection is a bounded view, not the thing itself.

Law 12 — No Agency Without Scope

Agents are projection-bound, admissibility-bound, authority-bound actors.

Law 13 — No Retention Without Decay / Review / Scope

Persistence must declare duration, echo eligibility, authority ceiling, and revocation or decay conditions.

Law 14 — No Semantics at T0

Meaning, value, policy, and interpretation belong to later bounded layers.

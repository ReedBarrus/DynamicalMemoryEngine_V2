# Dynamical Memory Engine — Collaborator Tiers and Authority Boundaries

## Status

This document defines a practical collaborator-tier framework for the Dynamical Memory Engine.

It is a supporting governance and collaboration note.

It does **not** override:

- `README.MasterConstitution.md`
- `README.WorkflowContract.md`
- `README/Constitution/Workflow/README.DevelopmentPressure.md`

Its purpose is narrower:

- define collaborator tiers by demonstrated relationship to the work,
- prevent projection, hype, or premature ownership from reshaping active scope,
- separate enthusiasm from authority,
- define lawful pathways to contribution,
- reduce social fusion and unclear participation pressure,
- preserve the difference between ideation, implementation, architecture, and ownership.

This note is about **project ecology and contribution posture**.
It is not a new runtime layer.
It is not a canon note.
It is not a legal ownership document.

---

## 1. Why this note exists

As DME becomes more visible, more people may want to participate.

Those people will not all relate to the work in the same way.

Some will:
- study the architecture,
- respect the governing notes,
- offer bounded practical value,
- and contribute inside lawful seams.

Others may:
- project hype,
- jump prematurely to product empire narratives,
- seek positional ownership before contribution,
- or flatten the architecture into whatever they already want the project to be.

This note exists to prevent that flattening.

The goal is not exclusion.
The goal is lawful relationship.

DME already depends on:
- boundary discipline,
- provenance discipline,
- scope discipline,
- and authority separation.

Its collaborator ecology should reflect the same posture.

---

## 2. Core rule

**Influence in the project is granted by demonstrated understanding, bounded contribution, and scope discipline — not by excitement, proximity, charisma, or projected future value.**

A collaborator’s place in the project is determined by:

- demonstrated understanding,
- actual contribution,
- boundary preservation,
- willingness to work at the lowest lawful layer,
- and ability to avoid silently inflating scope.

This follows the same general project posture that:
- runtime is not canon,
- query is not truth,
- substrate is not ontology,
- consensus is promotion-only,
- and higher-order pressure must not silently reshape active lower layers.

---

## 3. Collaboration posture

This framework assumes the following:

- not every interested person is an active contributor,
- not every contributor is an architectural collaborator,
- not every useful idea deserves current-scope influence,
- not every commercial instinct is a lawful roadmap input,
- and not every person who sees future potential has earned present authority.

The project must remain able to say:

- **yes**
- **not yet**
- **useful, but downstream**
- **helpful, but outside current seam**
- **contribute here, not there**

without social guilt being allowed to override lawful development.

---

## 4. Tier overview

The current collaborator tiers are:

- Tier 0 — Observer / Interested Party
- Tier 1 — Bounded Ideation Contributor
- Tier 2 — Applied Contributor
- Tier 3 — Trusted Domain / Implementation Partner
- Tier 4 — Architectural Collaborator

These tiers are not moral rankings.
They are **authority and scope bands**.

They exist to answer:

- what kind of influence does this person currently have,
- what kind of contribution are they ready for,
- what kind of pressure should not be admitted from them yet,
- and what path exists for them to move deeper lawfully.

---

## 5. Tier definitions

## 5.1 Tier 0 — Observer / Interested Party

### Definition

A person who is interested in the project, energized by it, or socially adjacent to it, but who has not yet demonstrated sufficient understanding or made a bounded contribution.

### Typical characteristics

- curious
- enthusiastic
- speculative
- asks broad questions
- may have ideas
- has not yet engaged deeply with repo/README architecture
- may still be projecting onto the project

### Allowed role

Tier 0 participants may:

- ask questions
- watch demos
- receive high-level explanation
- offer broad use-case ideas
- offer first impressions
- identify areas of personal interest

### Not allowed role

Tier 0 participants may not:

- influence architecture
- define roadmap
- claim ownership
- pressure current scope
- dismiss bounded proving grounds without understanding them
- speak publicly as if they are shaping the system
- impose product or symbolic narratives as if they outrank active law

### Advancement requirement

To move out of Tier 0, a person should be able to:

- accurately describe what Door One currently is and is not,
- engage at least one governing README or active demo surface,
- and propose one bounded contribution instead of one broad projected future.

---

## 5.2 Tier 1 — Bounded Ideation Contributor

### Definition

A person whose main value is product, UX, market, workflow, or conceptual ideation, but who is not yet in the implementation or architecture core.

### Typical characteristics

- may have useful product instincts
- may help identify audiences or user pains
- may see workflow opportunities
- may still over-project if not bounded
- may need explicit lane discipline

### Allowed role

Tier 1 participants may:

- propose product surfaces
- suggest user workflows
- identify customer or operator pain points
- help translate DME into bounded demos
- help frame B2B or consumer-facing concepts
- produce mockups, flow maps, or surface concepts

### Not allowed role

Tier 1 participants may not:

- redefine architecture
- reinterpret artifact meaning
- import Door Two / Door Three pressure into Door One by enthusiasm
- claim that use-case instinct outranks active constitutional posture
- collapse runtime, demo, consultation, canon, and product into one authority blob
- pressure premature go-to-market narratives over current system law

### Advancement requirement

To move out of Tier 1, a person should:

- produce one bounded useful output,
- revise ideas in response to active repo/README law,
- and consistently preserve scope discipline.

---

## 5.3 Tier 2 — Applied Contributor

### Definition

A person who can add real bounded value in an implementation, demo, packaging, domain, or application seam without needing to own or redefine the whole system.

### Typical characteristics

- capable of concrete work
- can contribute code, workflow, packaging, or domain utility
- willing to work inside declared seams
- respects review and sequence
- useful in real project movement

### Allowed role

Tier 2 participants may:

- contribute PRs
- help with demos
- help with adapters
- help with packaging
- help with deployment paths
- help with domain-facing workflows
- help define bounded pilot surfaces
- help translate DME into real use cases

### Not allowed role

Tier 2 participants may not:

- redefine constitutional meaning
- claim architecture authority by having shipped code
- smuggle hype or symbolic abstraction into active seams
- pressure commercialization paths beyond current phase law
- use contribution volume as an authority shortcut

### Advancement requirement

To move out of Tier 2, a person should:

- repeatedly demonstrate scope discipline,
- preserve architecture under pressure,
- contribute inside bounded seams without inflation,
- and show long-form reliability across more than one task cycle.

---

## 5.4 Tier 3 — Trusted Domain / Implementation Partner

### Definition

A person with a real skill lane, real reciprocal value, demonstrated scope discipline, and a repeated history of engaging the work without flattening it.

### Typical characteristics

- has a developed lane of their own
- contributes real practical value
- respects system law
- can participate in serious sequencing and demo strategy discussions
- does not treat excitement as authority

### Allowed role

Tier 3 participants may:

- help identify bounded pilot lanes
- help shape serious practical demos
- help define use-case priorities inside active scope
- help contribute implementation and packaging work
- help refine real-world surfaces for specific target domains
- participate in narrow roadmap discussion within the active phase

### Not allowed role

Tier 3 participants may not:

- override architecture
- redefine boundaries
- collapse contribution into ownership
- use practical usefulness as a reason to bulldoze future scope into the present
- silently shift the project away from constitutional law

### Advancement requirement

Tier 3 does not automatically advance to architectural collaboration.
That transition should remain rare and evidence-based.

---

## 5.5 Tier 4 — Architectural Collaborator

### Definition

A person who has repeatedly demonstrated deep understanding of the repo, governing notes, active Door posture, boundary law, and phase discipline over time.

### Typical characteristics

- accurate about current scope
- does not confuse runtime with canon
- does not project speculative layers downward
- can help reason about sequencing and legitimacy
- preserves architecture under ambiguity

### Allowed role

Tier 4 participants may:

- participate in architecture discussions
- raise bounded structural questions
- help evaluate seam legitimacy
- help identify drift before it lands in code or product pressure

### Not allowed role

Tier 4 participants may not:

- override Reed as project owner
- redefine the constitutional center unilaterally
- claim ownership through insight alone
- collapse architecture discussion into governance authority

### Special note

This tier should remain intentionally narrow.

Architectural collaboration is not a social reward.
It is a boundary-bearing function.

## 6. Authority bands by tier

The practical authority posture is:

| Tier | Practical Authority |
|---|---|
| Tier 0 | curiosity only |
| Tier 1 | bounded ideation only |
| Tier 2 | bounded contribution authority inside assigned seams |
| Tier 3 | trusted practical/strategic input inside declared active scope |
| Tier 4 | architecture discussion access, still below owner authority |

This table exists to reduce confusion.

A person may be highly intelligent, charismatic, visionary, or socially close to the project and still belong in a lower authority band if they have not yet demonstrated lawful relationship to the work.

## 7. Contribution pathways

The correct response to interest is not only tier assignment.
It is also a lawful path to contribute.

## 7.1 Path A — Learn first

Best for:
- Tier 0

Example tasks:
- read the project summary
- review one active README
- explain Door One in their own words
- identify one bounded practical use case
- ask clarification questions without projecting future layers downward

## 7.2 Path B — Product surface contribution

Best for:
- Tier 1

Example tasks:
- define a user workflow
- identify operator pain points
- propose a bounded product surface
- map input → runtime action → visible output
- produce a mockup without redefining architecture

## 7.3 Path C — Practical demo contribution

Best for:
- Tier 2 / Tier 3

Example tasks:
- help frame one departmental pilot
- define one target user and one bounded use case
- help package lawful outputs into a legible demo
- define what a real stakeholder sees and why it matters
- preserve provenance-first explanation

## 7.4 Path D — Code contribution

Best for:
- Tier 2+

Example tasks:
- PRs in bounded seams
- tests
- adapters
- scripts
- HUD/demo read-side surfaces
- implementation support under repo + README law

## 8. Red-flag behaviors

The following behaviors indicate projection pressure, authority confusion, or scope inflation risk.

### Behavioral red flags

- claiming ownership before contribution
- speaking as if vision alone earns authority
- dismissing bounded proving grounds as “too small” or “beneath the project”
- refusing to inspect repo/README posture while wanting strategic influence
- trying to collapse runtime, canon, demo, and commercialization into one story
- repeated hype language without bounded contribution
- treating enthusiasm as proof
- trying to universalize one downstream idea into the whole architecture
- smuggling symbolic, reputational, or empire logic into active runtime scope

### Interpretation rule

These red flags do not automatically mean a person is bad, hostile, or useless.

They mean:
**their current authority tier should remain low, and their contribution lane should be narrowed.**

---

## 9. Boundary enforcement rule

When someone exceeds their current lawful participation band, the preferred response is:

- narrow scope,
- clarify role,
- assign a bounded task,
- and keep authority separate from enthusiasm.

Do **not** respond first by:
- arguing metaphysics,
- arguing personal worth,
- or over-explaining the whole architecture.

Prefer:

1. name the seam,
2. name the lane,
3. state what is in-bounds,
4. state what is not in-bounds yet,
5. offer one lawful next step.

This preserves energy while preventing fusion.

## 10. Product and practical-surface posture

A collaborator may have useful product instincts without having current architecture authority.

This is especially important for:

- B2B surfaces
- consumer-facing interfaces
- workflow tooling
- orchestration/control shells
- public demo framing

The correct posture is:

- product ideation is welcome,
- practical use cases are valuable,
- bounded proving grounds are legitimate,
- and no product instinct outranks active scope law.

A bounded proving ground such as:
- finance
- operations
- HR
- trust/context workflow
- annotation lifecycle
- hallucination-management instrumentation
- structural memory retrieval
- provenance-bearing review surfaces

may be exactly the correct current use case even if a collaborator prefers more glamorous narratives.

The rule is simple:

**practical proving grounds are not spiritually lesser than broad vision.**
They are often the lawful path by which real scope is earned.

## 12. Use in live conversations

A simple live-use sentence:

> “Right now I’m separating curiosity, ideation, contribution, and architecture authority. Influence in the project comes from demonstrated understanding, bounded contribution, and scope discipline — not just excitement or future vision.”

A simple boundary sentence for ideation contributors:

> “I want your help on product surfaces and bounded workflows, but not on redefining the runtime or pushing broader strategic conclusions before the active seams are stabilized.”

A simple path-to-contribution sentence:

> “The best next step is to contribute one bounded output inside a clearly defined lane.”

## 13. Final rule

The collaborator framework should behave like the rest of DME:

- preserve boundary,
- preserve provenance,
- preserve role clarity,
- preserve reversibility,
- and avoid inflation.

The project should not fuse:
- excitement with authority,
- vision with contribution,
- contribution with ownership,
- or usefulness with constitutional control.

**Lawful relationship comes first.**
Everything else should earn its place.

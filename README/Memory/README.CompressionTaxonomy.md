## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough replay, retention, reconstruction, summary, review, memory, coherence, and identity pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer a recurring question:

**What kind of compression occurred here, what was preserved, what was lost, and what continuity claims remain lawful afterward?**

Without a bounded compression taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- reduction being mistaken for harmless simplification,
- usefulness being mistaken for preserved identity,
- coherence being mistaken for preserved invariance,
- compactness being mistaken for lawful memory,
- replay shape being mistaken for support-preserving continuity,
- and summaries or reduced representations outrunning what surviving distinctions actually justify.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

---

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, compression is not merely making something smaller. Compression is the bounded reduction, remapping, or consolidation of structure under a declared question, where the legitimacy of the result depends on which invariants, supports, distinctions, and continuity conditions remain preserved strongly enough for the same bounded question to stay answerable honestly.**

Corollary rules:

- smaller is not automatically lawful,
- cleaner is not automatically safer,
- smoother is not automatically more coherent,
- more usable is not automatically identity-preserving,
- and reduced representation is not automatically preserved support.

If compression destroys required invariants, collapses support below threshold, or changes the bounded question while preserving the old continuity language, the system must not silently preserve the stronger claim.

It must instead narrow, downgrade, defer, split, reclassify, or emit unresolved posture.

## 4. Definition of compression

In DME, **compression** means:

> the bounded reduction, condensation, remapping, summarization, or consolidation of structure, support, or representation under a declared question, where the result remains lawful only to the degree that required invariants, supports, and distinctions survive strongly enough for that same bounded question.

This means compression is always:

- question-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- support-relative,
- invariance-relative,
- coherence-relative,
- boundary-relative where crossing matters,
- and regime-relative under perturbation, retention, replay, or reconstruction.

Compression is **not** guaranteed to be lawful merely because:

- the result is shorter,
- the result is faster,
- the result is easier to inspect,
- the result looks coherent,
- the result remains somewhat useful,
- or the result resembles the source broadly.

## 5. Compression is not memory, coherence, invariance, or identity

Compression interacts with all of these, but is not reducible to any one of them.

### Memory
Memory asks:
- what remains preserved and reusable?

Compression asks:
- what was reduced, and what kind of memory claim survives the reduction?

### Coherence
Coherence asks:
- how strongly do the relevant parts still hold together?

Compression asks:
- what reduction pressure is acting on those parts, and what class of coherence survives afterward?

### Invariance
Invariance asks:
- what must not change?

Compression asks:
- which invariants were preserved, weakened, substituted, or destroyed?

### Identity
Identity asks:
- what bounded object remains the same?

Compression asks:
- did this reduction preserve the same bounded object, narrow it, degrade it, or break it?

One-line distinction:

**Compression is the pressure and process of reduction; invariance defines what must survive it, coherence measures how well the reduced object still holds together, memory measures what remains reusable, and identity measures what can still lawfully count as the same thing.**

## 6. The six compression classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes six primary compression classes.

---

### 6.1 K0 — Representational compression

#### Definition

Reduction in size, dimensionality, or explicitness of representation without yet making a stronger claim about preserved support or continuity.

#### Bounded question

**What has been made smaller or more compact at the representational level?**

#### Primary role

- reduce surface size,
- condense form,
- simplify presentation,
- change representation density.

#### Common examples

- shorter display objects,
- summarized views,
- reduced visual representation,
- compact transformed outputs.

#### Main failure modes

- representational flattening mistaken for safe continuity,
- decorative cleanliness,
- hidden loss behind compact shape.

#### One-line summary

**K0 is reduction of representation.**

---

### 6.2 K1 — Structural compression

#### Definition

Reduction that attempts to preserve relevant structure or invariant-bearing arrangement under the active bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**What structure is being preserved while representation is reduced?**

#### Primary role

- preserve relation-bearing shape,
- retain salient structure under reduction,
- support structural comparison after reduction.

#### Common examples

- reduced structural descriptors,
- compact neighborhood summaries,
- structural signatures,
- invariant-aware feature reduction.

#### Main failure modes

- false structural equivalence,
- topology loss,
- feature over-collapse,
- preserved resemblance without preserved structure.

#### One-line summary

**K1 is reduction with structural preservation intent.**

---

### 6.3 K2 — Support compression

#### Definition

Reduction that preserves enough lineage, receipts, support traces, or bounded evidence to keep later support questions answerable, even if the full surface no longer survives.

#### Bounded question

**Does enough support survive this reduction for the same support question to remain answerable?**

#### Primary role

- retain lineage-bearing handles,
- preserve support traces,
- support replay/review/audit at a weaker but still lawful level.

#### Common examples

- digests,
- compressed support packets,
- reduced replay traces,
- support summaries with explicit downgrade posture.

#### Main failure modes

- support inflation,
- digest mistaken for receipts,
- lineage weakening,
- retained residue mistaken for preserved support.

#### One-line summary

**K2 is reduction with bounded support preservation.**

---

### 6.4 K3 — Memory compression

#### Definition

Reduction that preserves enough structure and support for a bounded memory claim to remain lawful at some memory class.

#### Bounded question

**What class of memory still survives after this reduction?**

#### Primary role

- preserve reusability under reduction,
- support operational or narrower memory claims,
- distinguish memory-bearing compression from mere storage reduction.

#### Common examples

- retained structures that still answer the same narrow replay/review question,
- reduced forms that preserve M1 or M2,
- bounded adaptive reuse after modest reduction.

#### Main failure modes

- memory overclaim,
- compressed continuity mistaken for preserved operational memory,
- support preserved but memory claim broken,
- silent question widening.

#### One-line summary

**K3 is reduction with bounded memory preservation.**

---

### 6.5 K4 — Identity-sensitive compression

#### Definition

Reduction that is evaluated directly against identity continuity thresholds because the reduced result is being used to support same-object claims.

#### Bounded question

**Did this compression preserve the same bounded object, narrow it lawfully, or break identity?**

#### Primary role

- govern replay/reconstruction/reminting accountability,
- prevent compressed sameness inflation,
- support identity downgrade posture explicitly.

#### Common examples

- replay reduction used in same-object review,
- compressed workflow object being treated as the same packet/object,
- retained summaries used for continuity claims,
- support-trace reconstruction under reduced evidence.

#### Main failure modes

- false equivalence,
- distortion overrun,
- identity inflation,
- support collapse beneath same-object threshold,
- narrowed object presented as unchanged object.

#### One-line summary

**K4 is reduction under identity accountability.**

---

### 6.6 K5 — Cross-layer compression

#### Definition

Reduction that compresses across more than one relevant surface, layer, or support regime and therefore must preserve cross-layer alignment strongly enough for the bounded question to remain answerable.

#### Bounded question

**Did this reduction preserve cross-layer continuity, or only produce a compact stitched surface?**

#### Primary role

- govern multi-surface summaries,
- govern runtime-to-retention reductions,
- govern replay/review/consultation compaction,
- prevent separately valid fragments from being overread as one preserved whole.

#### Common examples

- runtime + retained summary objects,
- review packets built from multiple surfaces,
- cross-pane or cross-run compaction,
- dossier-like support condensation.

#### Main failure modes

- stitched-story inflation,
- cross-layer fragmentation hidden by compact summary,
- local coherence without whole-object preservation,
- support substitution between layers.

#### One-line summary

**K5 is reduction under cross-layer continuity pressure.**

## 7. Compression staircase

The six classes form a practical staircase:

- **K0** = representational compression
- **K1** = structural compression
- **K2** = support compression
- **K3** = memory compression
- **K4** = identity-sensitive compression
- **K5** = cross-layer compression

This staircase should remain explicit.

The system must not silently round weaker compression classes upward.

Examples:

- K0 is not automatically K1
- K1 is not automatically K2
- K2 is not automatically K3
- K3 is not automatically K4
- K4 is not automatically K5

## 8. Compression functions

Each compression class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 8.1 Reduction function

**What is being reduced?**

Examples:
- representation size,
- feature density,
- support breadth,
- replay detail,
- retained evidence,
- cross-surface explicitness.

### 8.2 Preservation function

**What must survive the reduction?**

Examples:
- invariants,
- structure,
- lineage,
- support traces,
- memory class,
- identity threshold,
- cross-layer alignment.

### 8.3 Failure function

**What exact break occurs when the reduction overreaches?**

Examples:
- flattening,
- support loss,
- memory downgrade,
- identity inflation,
- stitched false whole,
- unresolved posture.

This three-function view prevents compression from collapsing into vague “summarization” language.

## 9. Compression regimes

Compression classes do not operate in a vacuum.

The same reduction may be lawful or destructive depending on the active regime.

The current taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 9.1 R1 — Stable reduction regime

Compression occurs under steady conditions with the same bounded question clearly preserved.

Best fit:

- K0
- K1
- K2

Typical use:

- compact display shaping,
- stable summary reduction,
- same-lens support condensation.

---

### 9.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Compression occurs under mild variation, but the same bounded question remains active.

Best fit:

- K1
- K2
- K3
- K4

Typical use:

- bounded replay variation,
- moderate support reduction,
- same-family/local continuity under small change.

---

### 9.3 R3 — Compression-stress regime

Compression pressure is high and preservation thresholds are at risk.

Best fit:

- all classes, especially K2–K5

Typical danger:

- support flattening,
- invariance loss,
- false coherence,
- compressed continuity overclaim.

---

### 9.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

Compression occurs across changed lenses, interfaces, or retained tiers.

Best fit:

- K2
- K4
- K5

Typical danger:

- changed boundary mistaken for preserved same-object reduction,
- support substitution across layers.

---

### 9.5 R5 — Rupture regime

Compression has reduced structure strongly enough that the same bounded question is no longer answerable honestly.

Possible outcomes:

- degraded support,
- degraded memory,
- degraded identity,
- support-only posture,
- new object/class required,
- unresolved posture.

---

## 10. Noise, distortion, and uncertainty classification

Compression naturally pairs with three recurring pressure classes: noise, distortion, and uncertainty.

These should remain explicit.

---

### 10.1 N0 — Background noise

#### Definition

Non-salient variation or low-level interference that does not yet break the bounded question.

#### Risk under compression

Compression may safely reduce some background noise if required invariants and supports remain intact.

#### Main danger

Treating all noisy variation as disposable when some of it is actually structure-bearing under the active question.

#### One-line summary

**N0 is bounded low-salience variation.**

---

### 10.2 N1 — Ambiguous noise

#### Definition

Variation that may be noise or may contain relevant distinction, depending on lens, regime, or support question.

#### Risk under compression

Premature removal may destroy support, anomaly signals, or emerging structure.

#### Main danger

Noise classification by convenience rather than by bounded question.

#### One-line summary

**N1 is uncertainly classified variation.**

---

### 10.3 N2 — Adversarial noise

#### Definition

Variation that directly interferes with detection, support preservation, or lawful compression.

#### Risk under compression

May force false smoothness, spurious coherence, or support loss.

#### Main danger

Compressed output looks cleaner while becoming less truthful.

#### One-line summary

**N2 is variation that actively stresses lawful preservation.**

---

### 10.4 D0 — Benign distortion

#### Definition

Minor reduction-induced alteration that does not cross the threshold required by the active bounded question.

#### Main danger

Calling it harmless without checking the actual question and invariants.

#### One-line summary

**D0 is bounded distortion below threshold.**

---

### 10.5 D1 — Interpretive distortion

#### Definition

Alteration strong enough that later interpretation may be biased, simplified, or misdirected even if some support still survives.

#### Main danger

Preserving stronger continuity language because the object remains somewhat useful.

#### One-line summary

**D1 is distortion that misleads later reading.**

---

### 10.6 D2 — Identity distortion

#### Definition

Alteration strong enough that the same bounded identity question can no longer be answered honestly.

#### Main danger

Compressed same-object claims after the relevant identity threshold has already failed.

#### One-line summary

**D2 is distortion that breaks same-object continuity at the active class.**

---

### 10.7 U0 — Low uncertainty

#### Definition

Compression outcome remains classable with high confidence under the declared question and support basis.

#### One-line summary

**U0 is classable reduction with low ambiguity.**

---

### 10.8 U1 — Bounded uncertainty

#### Definition

Compression outcome is usable, but some preservation question remains unresolved or near-threshold.

#### Main danger

Rounding ambiguity upward into stronger continuity language.

#### One-line summary

**U1 is usable but threshold-sensitive reduction.**

---

### 10.9 U2 — High uncertainty

#### Definition

Compression outcome no longer supports confident classing at the active seam.

#### Main danger

Pretending clarity where only unresolved posture is lawful.

#### One-line summary

**U2 is reduction beyond confident classification.**

---

## 11. Compression is not simplification, usefulness, or cleanliness alone

Several surfaces can look better after compression while becoming less lawful.

### 11.1 Simpler is not automatically safer

Reduction may remove exactly the distinctions needed for the active bounded question.

### 11.2 More useful is not automatically more preserving

A reduced object may become easier to handle for a broader question while failing the original narrow question.

### 11.3 Cleaner is not automatically more coherent

Visual or narrative smoothness may hide support collapse or invariance loss.

### 11.4 Shorter is not automatically more honest

A compact object can overstate certainty by hiding what was removed.

## 12. Compression-bearing vs support-only posture

A central dynamics distinction is:

### Compression-bearing

A reduced object supports a real bounded compression claim at its current class.

### Support-only

The reduced object remains useful as support, display, memory trace, or review aid, but does not justify the stronger compression-preservation claim.

### Flattened residue

The reduced object still appears orderly or useful, but no longer preserves the relevant support, invariance, or identity class honestly.

This distinction should remain explicit.

The system must not casually call something well-compressed when it is only:

- cleaner,
- smaller,
- smoother,
- easier to narrate,
- or partially preserved.

## 14. Relation to coherence taxonomy

Coherence asks:

**How strongly do the relevant parts still hold together?**

This note asks:

**What reduction occurred, and what coherence class still survives afterward?**

The relationship is therefore:

- compression may preserve or weaken coherence,
- high coherence after compression is not sufficient by itself,
- and compression can produce false coherence by flattening distinctions.

Useful relation:

- K0 may preserve only H0
- K1 often targets H1
- K2–K3 often require H2–H3

## 15. Relation to memory taxonomy

Memory asks:

**What remains preserved and reusable across time?**

This note asks:

**Did the reduction preserve the memory class being claimed, or only a weaker support class?**

The relationship is therefore:

- compression often acts directly on memory-bearing objects,
- memory degradation can occur before total support loss,
- and adaptive memory claims are especially sensitive to compression overreach.

Useful relation:

- M0 may survive K0–K2
- M1 often requires at least K2 legality
- M2 often requires K3 or stronger
- M3 is especially sensitive to K3–K5 failure

## 16. Relation to identity hierarchy

**Did reduction preserve the same identity class, narrow it, degrade it, or break it?**

- K4 is the main compression class for I2-facing identity audit,
- higher identity classes require richer compression discipline than lower ones.

Useful relation:

- I2 is most directly pressured by K1–K5

## 19. Relation to declared vs mechanized and closure gate

A surface must not be called more compression-safe than the actual implementation path justifies.

Therefore:

- declared compression posture is not mechanized compression discipline,
- displayed summary is not support-preserving compression by default,
- replay-shaped reduced output is not automatically lawful replay support,
- compact review objects are not automatically minting-safe.

## 23. Compression failure modes

Compression most commonly fails through the following modes.

### 23.1 Flattening

Distinct structures collapse into one simpler but misleading object.

### 23.2 Invariant loss

Required preserved properties no longer survive reduction.

### 23.3 Support thinning

Lineage, receipts, or support traces weaken below lawful threshold.

### 23.4 Memory inflation

A reduced object is treated as though it preserved a stronger memory class than it actually did.

### 23.5 Identity inflation

A reduced object is treated as though it preserved the same bounded object when the relevant threshold already failed.

### 23.6 Stitching illusion

Cross-layer reduction creates a compact but falsely unified story from separately valid fragments.

### 23.7 Noise misclassification

Relevant structure is discarded as noise by convenience.

### 23.8 Uncertainty suppression

Ambiguity is hidden by a cleaner summary rather than carried forward honestly.

The governing rule is:

**when a reduced object can no longer support the stronger compression claim honestly, the system must weaken the claim rather than preserve stronger language for convenience.**

## 27. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger compression claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

1. the compression class is explicit,
2. the same bounded question remains active,
3. the same declared constraints remain active,
4. required invariants are preserved strongly enough,
5. enough support survives for the relevant preservation claim,
6. any required memory or identity class remains lawful after reduction,
7. noise, distortion, and uncertainty are carried forward honestly,
8. and any required implementation path is actually mechanized.

If not, the reduced object may still be useful.

But it must not silently preserve the stronger compression class.

## 28. One-line practical rule

In DME, compression is lawful only to the degree that the required invariants, supports, and distinctions survive reduction strongly enough for the same bounded question to remain answerable; when they do not, the system must downgrade the claim rather than preserve stronger continuity language by convenience.

## 29. One-line summary

The DME compression taxonomy distinguishes representational, structural, support, memory, identity-sensitive, and cross-layer compression, and pairs them with explicit noise, distortion, and uncertainty classes so reduction can be discussed with bounded honesty, explicit downgrade posture, and clear linkage to coherence, invariance, memory, and identity.

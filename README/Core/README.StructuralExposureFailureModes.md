## 1. Why this note exists

`README.StructuralExposureLaw.md` defines what a lawful structural exposure surface must preserve.

But DME now has enough runtime, replay, retention, semantic, workflow, visualization, and packet pressure that lawful exposure can fail in multiple distinct ways even when a surface still appears coherent.

These failures are especially dangerous because they often present as improvement:

- more readable,
- more helpful,
- more organized,
- more inspectable,
- more complete,
- or more human-friendly.

The risk is that such improvements can silently replace direct structural inspectability with semantic closure, support/accounting substitution, or review posture.

This note exists to define those failures explicitly.

One-line summary:

**A structural exposure failure occurs when the system appears to reveal a structure more clearly while actually reducing the operator’s direct access to the structure itself.**

---

## 2. Core rule

**Structural exposure failures in DME are not merely aesthetic mistakes. They are failures in which numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing structure becomes less directly inspectable because another layer has substituted, compressed, gated, or overshadowed it.**

Corollary rules:

- clearer is not automatically more exposed,
- more labeled is not automatically more inspectable,
- more summarized is not automatically more structural,
- more organized is not automatically more truthful to the seam,
- and more helpful is not automatically safer.

If a surface can no longer answer the same bounded structural question directly, it has failed as an exposure surface even if it remains useful in some other way.

## 4. Definition of structural exposure failure

In DME, a **structural exposure failure** means:

> any bounded shift in a surface, name, packet, display, or implementation path that makes numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing structure less directly inspectable for the active structural question, especially by replacing structure with labels, counts, summaries, semantic posture, or mixed-surface closure.

This means exposure failure is always:

- question-relative,
- surface-relative,
- naming-sensitive,
- layer-sensitive,
- drift-sensitive,
- and authority-sensitive.

A structural exposure failure is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- the surface is minimal,
- the surface is ugly,
- the surface lacks semantics,
- the surface lacks summaries,
- or the output requires technical interpretation.

The problem is not difficulty.
The problem is **substitution**.

One-line distinction:

**Hard-to-read structure may still be lawful exposure. Easy-to-read semantics may still be exposure failure.**

## 5. Failure families

The current bounded analysis recognizes five major failure families.

---

### 5.1 Naming-induced failure

A surface fails because its name silently biases design and interpretation toward semantic helpfulness rather than first-order exposure.

Typical pathway:

- a raw structural surface is named as if it were a human-friendly interpretation surface,
- implementation follows the name,
- the surface fills with labels, summaries, or states,
- raw structure becomes secondary or absent.

Examples:

- calling a raw structural surface a "viewer"
- calling a transform-space output an "inspector"
- calling a structural exposure surface a "dashboard"
- calling a numeric trace surface a "memory map"

Main risk:

The name acts as an undeclared control input on representation choice.

One-line summary:

**The surface was named for what humans like, not for what the seam must expose.**

---

### 5.2 Semantic substitution failure

A surface fails because semantics replace the structure they were supposed to annotate.

Typical pathway:

- labels, states, or summaries appear,
- the labels become the primary visible artifact,
- the underlying bins, arrays, coordinates, or relations disappear or become inaccessible,
- semantic posture becomes required to understand what is present.

Examples:

- "stable" replacing direct transform output
- "anomalous" replacing the actual geometric or numeric disturbance
- memory or identity posture appearing before raw structural output
- "structure exists" shown only through prose or categories

Main risk:

The operator can no longer inspect the underlying structure directly.

One-line summary:

**The system says what the structure means instead of showing the structure.**

---

### 5.3 Support/accounting overwrite failure

A surface fails because support, lineage, counts, handles, tiers, or bookkeeping dominate the surface and crowd out direct exposure.

Typical pathway:

- a structural surface is mixed with object counts, handles, receipts, tiers, or workflow references,
- support/accounting becomes the most visible layer,
- structure is implied through bookkeeping rather than exposed directly,
- the operator sees artifacts-about-structure instead of structure.

Examples:

- H1/M1 counts shown without raw bins or distributions
- lineage handles shown without transform-space exposure
- retention/support posture dominating a geometric surface
- object accounting standing in for direct comparison

Main risk:

The surface becomes precise without remaining structurally inspectable.

One-line summary:

**The system tracks the structure instead of exposing it.**

---

### 5.4 Mixed-surface collapse

A surface fails because multiple surface roles cohabit one display and the highest semantic or evaluative layer dominates.

Typical pathway:

- raw structure, support metadata, semantic overlay, and review posture are shown together,
- the surface tries to be useful at all layers simultaneously,
- the interpretive layer becomes cognitively primary,
- first-order exposure is still technically present or recoverable but is no longer functionally primary.

Examples:

- one panel trying to be raw exposure + support + review + readiness
- a replay display carrying raw output, semantic labels, and consultation posture at once
- structural plots surrounded by stronger class language that dictates interpretation before inspection

Main risk:

Even if raw structure is still present, it is no longer the surface’s true center of gravity.

One-line summary:

**Too many lawful layers in one place produce an unlawful exposure surface.**

---

### 5.5 Packet / prose drift failure

A surface fails indirectly because workflow packets, prose instructions, or helper framing shift the intended seam before implementation begins.

Typical pathway:

- the packet asks for a "viewer" or "insight" surface,
- the query class silently widens,
- the implementer or helper optimizes for interpretation,
- the resulting surface faithfully implements the wrong seam.

Examples:

- "make a structural viewer" becoming "make a semantic summary panel"
- "show geometry" becoming "show object states"
- "expose transform output" becoming "show transformed artifact counts"
- a packet asking for raw structure but using review- or semantic-heavy vocabulary

Main risk:

The failure enters before code or rendering decisions are even made.

One-line summary:

**The surface drifted in language before it drifted in implementation.**

## 6. The core failure modes

The following concrete failure modes should be tracked explicitly.

---

### 6.1 Geometric occlusion

#### Definition

Raw geometric, numeric, or relation-bearing output is absent, minimized, or visually subordinate.

#### Primary symptom

The operator cannot directly see bins, coordinates, arrays, field values, or relation-bearing structure.

#### Common causes

- semantic prioritization
- support/accounting dominance
- mixed-surface clutter
- naming-induced drift

#### Immediate containment

Expose raw output directly before any additional layer.

#### One-line summary

**The geometry is gone.**

---

### 6.2 Semantic overwrite

#### Definition

Labels, classes, or semantic summaries become the primary representation of the surface.

#### Primary symptom

Understanding the surface requires reading semantic posture before seeing structure.

#### Common causes

- early semantic closure
- overlay becoming primary
- structural output not rendered directly

#### Immediate containment

Remove semantic posture from first-order exposure surfaces.

#### One-line summary

**Meaning arrived before exposure.**

---

### 6.3 Compression camouflage

#### Definition

A surface appears cleaner or simpler, but only because distinctions required for the bounded structural question were flattened.

#### Primary symptom

The surface looks tidier while exposing less structure.

#### Common causes

- representational simplification
- helpful summarization
- compact object shaping
- count substitution

#### Immediate containment

Ask: what distinctions became invisible when the surface became cleaner?

#### One-line summary

**The surface got cleaner by losing structure.**

---

### 6.4 Mechanization camouflage

#### Definition

A polished or coherent surface creates the impression that raw structural exposure exists even when the underlying structural path is partial, inaccessible, or absent.

#### Primary symptom

The operator assumes the raw seam is available because the output surface is coherent.

#### Common causes

- strong UI shape
- object richness
- packet prose overreach
- display treated as proof of first-order availability

#### Immediate containment

Require direct export of raw numeric/geometric output.

#### One-line summary

**Surface polish is being mistaken for exposure attainment.**

---

### 6.5 Label gravity cascade

#### Definition

One or two small labels pull an entire surface upward into a stronger semantic, review, or authority posture than intended.

#### Primary symptom

A surface that began as raw exposure gradually accumulates heavier labels until the structural role is no longer primary.

#### Common causes

- labels treated as harmless
- no admission gates
- mixed-purpose additions
- no naming review

#### Immediate containment

Remove all non-essential labels and re-test whether the structural question remains directly answerable.

#### One-line summary

**Small labels caused large drift.**

---

### 6.6 Query drift by interface

#### Definition

The interface or surface answers a different class of question than the one the user or packet actually asked.

#### Primary symptom

A request for structural exposure receives support/accounting, semantic, or review output.

#### Common causes

- ambiguous naming
- mixed query vocabulary
- packet under-specification
- helper optimization toward usefulness

#### Immediate containment

Restate the active question in structural-only terms and rebuild surface role from that question.

#### One-line summary

**The surface is answering the wrong question well.**

---

### 6.7 Exposure inversion

#### Definition

The lawful rendering order is reversed so that semantics, support, or review appear before raw structure.

#### Primary symptom

The first visible layer is not first-order structure.

#### Common causes

- app composition for convenience
- semantic-first defaults
- surface mixing
- workflow pressure to show “what matters” first

#### Immediate containment

Restore lawful order:
1. raw structure
2. support/accounting
3. semantic overlay
4. review posture

#### One-line summary

**The stack is upside down.**

---

## 7. Failure diagnostics

When diagnosing a suspected exposure failure, ask the following in order.

### 7.1 First-order inspectability test

Can the active structural question be answered directly from numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing output?

If no:
- exposure failure is active.

### 7.2 Substitution test

What is the operator seeing **instead of** the raw structure?

Likely substitutions:
- labels
- counts
- handles
- summaries
- review posture
- readiness posture
- workflow status

### 7.3 Surface role test

Is this really a raw structural exposure surface, or has it become:

- support/accounting,
- semantic overlay,
- review,
- or mixed surface?

### 7.4 Naming test

Did the surface name bias implementation toward semantic closure?

### 7.5 Rendering-order test

Which layer appears first?

If the answer is anything other than raw structure:
- inversion pressure is active.

### 7.6 Export test

Can raw output be directly exported?

If no:
- mechanization camouflage or exposure failure is likely active.

## 8. Failure severity posture

Not all failures are equally severe.

Suggested bounded severity scale:

### E0 — No meaningful exposure failure

Raw structure remains primary and directly inspectable.

### E1 — Mild contamination

Minor labels or support metadata are present, but raw exposure remains primary.

### E2 — Structural occlusion risk

Raw exposure exists, but support/semantics/mixed-surface pressure is beginning to dominate.

### E3 — Functional exposure failure

The active structural question can no longer be answered directly.

### E4 — Exposure inversion / seam corruption

The surface claims or appears to be structural exposure while functioning primarily as semantic, support, or review surface.

One-line summary:

**E3 and E4 should trigger explicit correction, not quiet tolerance.**

---

## 9. Containment and repair strategies

When exposure failure is detected, lawful containment should prefer the smallest repair that restores first-order inspectability.

### 9.1 Split the surface

Separate:

- raw structural exposure
- support/accounting
- semantic overlay
- review posture

### 9.2 Rename the surface

Replace drift-inducing names with first-order functional names.

### 9.3 Remove labels

Temporarily strip all labels except axis/index/lens labels and test again.

### 9.4 Restore raw export path

Make raw arrays, bins, coordinates, or field values directly exportable.

### 9.5 Narrow the packet question

Reframe the active packet in structural-only language.

### 9.6 Reclassify honestly

If the surface is no longer structural exposure, stop calling it that.

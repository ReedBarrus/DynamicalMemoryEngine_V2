## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough runtime, replay, retention, review, semantic, workflow, and visualization pressure that one specific failure mode has become active:

**semantic helpfulness can erase structural inspectability before the structure has been directly exposed.**

This can happen even when the system appears coherent and productive.

Examples include:

- a geometric or numeric surface being replaced by labels, counts, or summaries,
- a request for raw structure being answered with semantic categories,
- a structural display being mixed with support/accounting/review posture until the raw structure is no longer directly visible,
- a surface name such as "viewer" or "inspector" silently pulling implementation toward semantic readability rather than first-order exposure,
- or a structural result being accessible only through interpretive prose rather than through direct numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing output.

This note exists because DME depends on a strict order:

**structure must become directly inspectable before interpretation is allowed to close over it.**

If this order fails, the system can appear rich while becoming less real.

One-line summary:

**Structural exposure must remain primary. Semantic closure must remain downstream, removable, and non-authoritative.**

---

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, a structural surface is lawful only when the bounded structural question can be directly inspected through numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing output without requiring semantic mediation.**

Corollary rules:

- labels are not exposure,
- counts are not exposure,
- summaries are not exposure,
- semantic posture is not exposure,
- support/accounting metadata is not exposure,
- review posture is not exposure,
- helpfulness is not automatically inspectability,
- and readable is not automatically structure-preserving.

If a surface requires semantic interpretation in order for the underlying structure to be seen at all, that surface is not functioning as a structural exposure surface.

It must instead be reclassified as:

- semantic overlay,
- support/accounting surface,
- review surface,
- or mixed surface requiring separation.

## 4. Definition of structural exposure

In DME, **structural exposure** means:

> the direct bounded availability of numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing output strongly enough that the active structural question can be inspected without requiring labels, summaries, semantic classes, or interpretive closure.

This means structural exposure is always:

- question-relative,
- surface-relative,
- lens-relative,
- support-relative where relevant,
- mechanization-relative where a real path matters,
- and regime-relative under perturbation, compression, drift, or boundary shift.

Structural exposure is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- a surface is visible,
- a UI looks clean,
- counts are shown,
- object handles are shown,
- semantic states are shown,
- a plot has labels,
- or a human says the surface feels understandable.

One-line distinction:

**Structural exposure is not what can be narrated about a structure. It is what can be directly inspected before narration.**

---

## 5. Structural exposure is not semantics, support, or review

These are related, but not the same.

### Structural exposure

A **first-order inspectability class**.

It answers:

- what structure is directly visible here?
- what numeric, geometric, or relation-bearing output is actually exposed?
- can the active structural question be inspected without interpretation?

### Semantic overlay

A **late-bound interpretive class**.

It answers:

- what bounded reading may be attached after the structure is already inspectable?

### Support / accounting

A **lineage and bookkeeping class**.

It answers:

- what handles, receipts, tiers, counts, or lineage traces exist?

### Review

An **evaluation class**.

It answers:

- how should this object be classified, narrowed, downgraded, or routed?

Therefore:

- structural exposure may exist without semantics,
- structural exposure may exist without support/accounting,
- structural exposure may exist without review,
- semantic overlay may be useful while still failing to expose structure,
- support/accounting may be precise while still failing to expose structure,
- and review posture may be correct while still failing to expose structure.

One-line distinction:

**Structural exposure shows the structure. Semantics describe it. Support tracks it. Review evaluates it.**

## 6. The Structural Exposure Law

A surface may be called a **structural exposure surface** only when all of the following are true:

1. **first-order output is directly exposed**
2. **semantic mediation is not required**
3. **labels do not substitute for structure**
4. **surface authority stays local**
5. **mixed-surface contamination is absent or explicitly separated**

### 6.1 First-order output is directly exposed

The surface must expose one or more of the following directly:

- numeric arrays,
- bins,
- coordinates,
- transform-space distributions,
- field values,
- relation-bearing plots,
- geometric traces,
- declared-lens output values,
- or equivalent first-order structure.

A count of objects is not enough.

A label is not enough.

A summary is not enough.

### 6.2 Semantic mediation is not required

The operator must be able to inspect the bounded structural question without needing:

- top-line labels,
- semantic state classes,
- descriptive prose,
- memory/identity/support posture language,
- or interpretation reports.

If semantics must be read before the structure can be seen, the surface fails.

### 6.3 Labels do not substitute for structure

If labels are present, they must not stand in for the underlying structure.

A label may point to an exposed distinction.

It may not replace it.

Examples:

Allowed:
- `dominant_bin=170`
- `window_index=42`
- `peak_magnitude=0.83`

Not allowed on a first-order structural surface:
- `stable`
- `broken`
- `memory-bearing`
- `insufficient support`
- `same object`
- `anomalous state`
- `healthy pattern`

These may belong to later surfaces, not first-order structural exposure.

### 6.4 Surface authority stays local

A structural exposure surface must remain below:

- support claims,
- memory claims,
- identity claims,
- review claims,
- consultation claims,
- promotion or canon posture.

The surface may expose structure that later supports such questions.

It may not silently answer them.

### 6.5 Mixed-surface contamination is absent or explicitly separated

A structural exposure surface must not cohabit by default with:

- semantic overlay,
- support/accounting dominance,
- review posture,
- workflow packet status,
- consultation readiness,
- or canon-adjacent interpretation.

If these appear at all, they must remain:

- visually separated,
- role-separated,
- removable,
- and downstream of raw exposure.

---

## 7. Semantic Non-Contact Rule

**Semantic overlays may read from structural surfaces, but may not define, replace, compress, gate, or stand in for first-order structural inspectability.**

Corollary rules:

- semantic posture may not be the first visible layer on a structural surface,
- semantic summaries may not replace arrays, bins, coordinates, or field values,
- semantic states may not become the primary artifact when the structural question is still active,
- interpretation may not be required to recover the underlying structure,
- and a structural surface may not be named or shaped in a way that silently invites semantic substitution.

Failure examples:

- "viewer" surfaces that show counts and labels but no raw bins,
- object/state dashboards treated as geometric viewers,
- semantic summaries presented before raw transform output,
- or first-order structure only accessible through prose.

One-line summary:

**Semantics may annotate structure. They may not become the structure’s first visible form.**

---

## 8. Label admission gates

Labels are not forbidden.

But every label is load-bearing and must pass explicit gates before appearing on a structural surface.

A label is lawful on a structural surface only if it passes all four gates below.

### 8.1 Exposure Preservation Gate

**Does the label preserve direct inspectability of the underlying structure?**

If removing the label leaves structural inspectability intact, the label may still be admissible.

If the label is carrying the interpretive burden of making the structure visible at all, it fails.

### 8.2 Non-Substitution Gate

**Is the label describing an exposed distinction rather than standing in for it?**

Allowed:
- index labels,
- axis labels,
- declared-lens labels,
- coordinate markers,
- direct numeric descriptors.

Not allowed:
- semantic or evaluative replacements for unshown structure.

### 8.3 Authority Alignment Gate

**Is the label staying within the authority of the active surface?**

A raw transform or geometric surface may carry:
- axis names,
- bin ids,
- declared lens,
- numeric descriptors.

It may not carry:
- support class,
- memory class,
- identity class,
- review class,
- readiness posture,
- or semantic verdict posture.

### 8.4 Scope Containment Gate

**Does the label remain inside the active bounded question?**

If the active question is:

- what bins are exposed?
- what relation is visible?
- what transform output exists?

then labels that imply:
- interpretation,
- continuity,
- evaluation,
- mechanism maturity,
- or broader class closure

must not appear.

One-line summary:

**A label is lawful only when it preserves exposure, avoids substitution, stays inside surface authority, and remains inside the active bounded question.**

## 9. Surface classes

DME should keep the following surface classes explicit.

### 9.1 Raw structural exposure surface

Allowed content:

- arrays,
- bins,
- coordinates,
- transform-space plots,
- field values,
- relation traces,
- declared lens,
- minimal axis/index labels,
- direct numeric descriptors.

Forbidden content:

- semantic classes,
- memory/identity claims,
- support verdicts,
- review posture,
- consultation posture,
- packet status,
- canon-adjacent readiness.

### 9.2 Support / accounting surface

Allowed content:

- handles,
- counts,
- receipts,
- lineage,
- retained tier,
- support traces,
- packet references.

This is not a structural exposure surface.

### 9.3 Semantic overlay surface

Allowed content:

- bounded interpretation,
- descriptor-backed posture,
- caution posture,
- downgrade-aware labels,
- removable overlays.

This must remain downstream of raw structural exposure.

### 9.4 Review surface

Allowed content:

- classification,
- narrowing,
- downgrade posture,
- unresolved posture,
- consultation blockers,
- readiness discussion.

This must remain separate from raw exposure.

---

## 10. Rendering order rule

Where multiple surfaces are shown in one operator flow, the lawful order is:

1. **raw structural exposure**
2. **support / accounting metadata**
3. **optional semantic overlay**
4. **optional review / consultation posture**

This order must remain explicit.

A surface stack that begins with semantics, support classes, or review posture before raw exposure is at risk of structural occlusion.

---

## 11. Naming law for structural surfaces

Names are not neutral.

A surface name acts as a control input on what the surface tends to optimize for.

Therefore a structural exposure surface must be named according to its first-order function, not according to human convenience posture.

### 11.1 Preferred naming posture

Names should preserve ugly clarity when necessary.

Prefer names such as:

- raw transform surface
- bin exposure surface
- declared-lens output
- structural field plot
- numeric relation surface
- geometric trace surface

### 11.2 High-risk names

The following names are risky on first-order structural surfaces because they silently invite semantic closure:

- viewer
- dashboard
- inspector
- memory map
- state viewer
- insight panel

These names are not forbidden everywhere.

They are high-risk for first-order structural exposure seams.

### 11.3 Naming rule

If a surface name implies readability, explanation, helpfulness, or interpretation more strongly than direct exposure, the name should be treated as drift risk and reviewed before use.

---

## 12. Validation checks

A structural exposure surface should fail validation if any of the following are true:

- raw numeric or geometric output cannot be directly exported,
- arrays / bins / coordinates / field values are absent,
- semantic labels are required to understand what is present,
- counts are the primary visible proxy for structure,
- support/accounting dominates the surface,
- review posture dominates the surface,
- first-order geometry is inaccessible,
- removing labels leaves no inspectable structure behind,
- or the surface answers a review or semantic question while claiming to be structural exposure.

---

## 13. Failure posture

If a surface fails this law, it must not silently remain classified as a structural exposure surface.

It must instead be reclassified honestly as one of:

- semantic overlay surface,
- support/accounting surface,
- review surface,
- mixed surface,
- or unresolved surface requiring separation.

If needed, the correct response is:

- split the surface,
- rename the surface,
- remove labels,
- expose raw output directly,
- or narrow the surface’s declared role.

## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough replay, inspection, retention, identity, and compression pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer two recurring questions:

**What does it mean for a structure to be observable?**  
**What does it mean for a boundary interaction to count as feedback rather than mere contact or disturbance?**

Without a bounded observability and feedback taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- exposure being mistaken for observability,
- displayed surfaces being mistaken for registered state,
- replay visibility being mistaken for actual observation,
- perturbation being mistaken for lawful feedback,
- response being mistaken for meaningful state coupling,
- and coherence language outrunning what the system actually registers and uses.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

## 2. Core rules

### 2.1 Observability rule

**In DME, observability is not mere exposure. Observability is bounded detectability of structure or change under declared constraints strongly enough that the system can register it without inventing support.**

Corollary rules:

- visible is not automatically observable,
- replay-shaped is not automatically observed,
- similarity is not automatically detectability,
- contact is not automatically registration,
- and registration is not automatically interpretation.

### 2.2 Feedback rule

**In DME, feedback is not mere disturbance. Feedback is lawful boundary-mediated interference in which external or adjacent state affects a bounded structure, that effect is registered strongly enough to alter future state, and the resulting change remains classable under declared constraints.**

Corollary rules:

- perturbation is not automatically feedback,
- amplification is not automatically useful feedback,
- response is not automatically stable feedback,
- and repeated interaction is not automatically adaptive coupling.

## 4. Definitions

### 4.1 Observability

In DME, **observability** means:

> the bounded ability for structure, change, perturbation, or relation to become detectably available under declared constraints strongly enough that it can be registered without invented support.

Observability is always:

- question-relative,
- boundary-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- support-relative,
- and regime-relative.

### 4.2 Registration

**Registration** means:

> the bounded internal preservation of an observed distinction strongly enough that it can participate in later accounting, comparison, memory, or state change.

Registration is stronger than raw exposure but weaker than interpretation.

### 4.3 Feedback

**Feedback** means:

> lawful boundary-mediated interference in which a registered interaction influences future internal or cross-boundary state.

Feedback requires:

- contact or coupling,
- detectability,
- registration,
- and downstream state consequence.

### 4.4 Observability is not feedback

A structure can be observable without producing meaningful feedback.

A structure can also be perturbed without the perturbation becoming a feedback signal the system can lawfully use.

One-line distinction:

**Observability makes distinctions available. Feedback makes registered distinctions matter for future state.**

## 5. Observability is not identity or memory

Observability, memory, and identity are related but not the same.

### Observability
A **detectability class**.

It answers:
- can something be sensed,
- registered,
- or distinguished under declared constraints?

### Memory
A **continuity support class**.

It answers:
- what remains preserved and reusable across time?

### Identity
A **continuity class**.

It answers:
- what bounded structure remains the same under declared constraints?

Therefore:

- observability can occur without memory,
- memory can persist without present observability,
- identity can remain while observability weakens,
- and observable change is not automatically identity change.

## 6. The four observability classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes four primary observability classes.

---

### 6.1 O0 — Exposure

#### Definition

Structure or change is present at a boundary or surface, but not yet classably registered.

#### Bounded question

**Was anything available to be encountered at all?**

#### Constraint relation

Boundary contact only.

#### Primary role

- raw availability,
- surface contact,
- possible precursor to observation.

#### Not enough for

- actual observation,
- registration,
- comparison,
- or feedback.

#### Main failure modes

- invisibility,
- occlusion,
- contact without detectability,
- decorative exposure.

#### One-line summary

**O0 is available contact without bounded registered observation.**

---

### 6.2 O1 — Detectable observability

#### Definition

Structure or change is detectably available under declared constraints strongly enough that it can be said to have been observed at a bounded level.

#### Bounded question

**Can this distinction be detected at all under the active boundary and constraint conditions?**

#### Constraint relation

Boundary + detectability constraints.

#### Primary role

- bounded sensing,
- distinction availability,
- first lawful observation class.

#### Not enough for

- durable registration,
- feedback coupling,
- or interpretive reuse by itself.

#### Main failure modes

- weak signal,
- threshold miss,
- spurious detectability,
- unstable sensing.

#### One-line summary

**O1 is bounded detectable observation.**

---

### 6.3 O2 — Registered observability

#### Definition

Detected structure or change is preserved internally strongly enough to participate in later accounting, comparison, memory, or state evaluation.

#### Bounded question

**Was the observed distinction actually registered strongly enough to matter later?**

#### Constraint relation

Detectability + internal registration + support sufficiency.

#### Primary role

- state accounting,
- anomaly registration,
- comparison readiness,
- memory precursor.

#### Not enough for

- stable adaptive feedback by itself,
- or interpretation beyond the declared use.

#### Main failure modes

- transient detection without registration,
- registration loss,
- false positives,
- internal collapse of distinction.

#### One-line summary

**O2 is observed and registered distinction.**

---

### 6.4 O3 — Operative observability

#### Definition

Registered observation is strong enough to lawfully shape future state, routing, weighting, or continuity posture under the same bounded question and declared constraints.

#### Bounded question

**Can the registered observation now influence future state or decision at the same bounded seam?**

#### Constraint relation

Registration + lawful state consequence + bounded same-question use.

#### Primary role

- state adjustment,
- anomaly response,
- weighting change,
- continuity/accounting effect,
- feedback activation.

#### Main failure modes

- mechanization gap,
- ornamental registration,
- unstable influence,
- overread significance.

#### One-line summary

**O3 is registered observation that can lawfully shape future state.**

## 7. The four feedback classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes four primary feedback classes.

---

### 7.1 F0 — Contact disturbance

#### Definition

A boundary is perturbed, but the perturbation is not detectably or usefully registered.

#### Bounded question

**Was there any contact or disturbance at all?**

#### Constraint relation

Boundary contact only.

#### Primary role

- raw perturbation,
- pre-feedback disturbance.

#### Not enough for

- observation,
- registration,
- or state-guiding feedback.

#### Main failure modes

- unnoticed disturbance,
- absorbed noise,
- decorative perturbation.

#### One-line summary

**F0 is contact without feedback classability.**

---

### 7.2 F1 — Registered feedback

#### Definition

A perturbation or interaction is detected and registered as a bounded signal that can be accounted for later.

#### Bounded question

**Did the interaction become a registered feedback signal?**

#### Constraint relation

Contact + detectability + registration.

#### Primary role

- interference accounting,
- anomaly signal,
- response precursor.

#### Not enough for

- stable future-state shaping,
- adaptation,
- or robust regulation by itself.

#### Main failure modes

- noisy registration,
- false coupling,
- unstable signal.

#### One-line summary

**F1 is disturbance that became registered as feedback.**

---

### 7.3 F2 — State-shaping feedback

#### Definition

Registered feedback alters future state, weighting, boundary handling, or continuity posture under the same bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**Did the registered feedback actually change future system state lawfully?**

#### Constraint relation

Registration + lawful causal consequence.

#### Primary role

- weighting shifts,
- state adjustment,
- boundary sensitivity changes,
- continuity modulation.

#### Main failure modes

- decorative response,
- overreaction,
- underreaction,
- unstable state shaping.

#### One-line summary

**F2 is feedback that changes future state.**

---

### 7.4 F3 — Adaptive feedback

#### Definition

State-shaping feedback remains lawful across bounded variation and improves or preserves continuity without silently widening the bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**Can the system use feedback across bounded variation without losing identity of function?**

#### Constraint relation

State consequence + bounded adaptation + same-question preservation.

#### Primary role

- adaptive regulation,
- bounded robustness,
- lawful response under perturbation.

#### Main failure modes

- runaway amplification,
- rigid overcorrection,
- scope inflation,
- false adaptation,
- identity drift disguised as robustness.

#### One-line summary

**F3 is bounded adaptive feedback.**

## 8. Observability and feedback staircases

### 8.1 Observability staircase

- **O0** = exposed
- **O1** = detectable
- **O2** = registered
- **O3** = operative

### 8.2 Feedback staircase

- **F0** = contact disturbance
- **F1** = registered feedback
- **F2** = state-shaping feedback
- **F3** = adaptive feedback

The system must not silently round weaker classes upward.

Examples:

- O0 is not automatically O1
- O1 is not automatically O2
- O2 is not automatically O3
- F0 is not automatically F1
- F1 is not automatically F2
- F2 is not automatically F3

## 9. Observability functions

Each observability class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 9.1 Access function

**What can cross or present at the boundary?**

### 9.2 Distinction function

**What can actually be discriminated under current constraints?**

### 9.3 Registration function

**What observed distinction survives strongly enough to matter later?**

This three-function view prevents observability from collapsing into mere visibility.

---

## 10. Feedback functions

Each feedback class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 10.1 Coupling function

**What kind of interaction occurs across the boundary?**

### 10.2 Response function

**What internal change follows from that interaction?**

### 10.3 Consequence function

**Does that response alter future state, weighting, or continuity posture?**

This three-function view prevents feedback from collapsing into mere perturbation language.

## 11. Regimes of observability and feedback

Observability and feedback can succeed or fail differently depending on the active regime.

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 11.1 R1 — Stable sensing regime

Boundary conditions are steady and observation is repeatable.

Best fit:

- O1
- O2
- F1

Typical use:

- stable replay inspection,
- repeated anomaly sensing,
- local runtime comparability.

---

### 11.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Small lawful changes occur, but distinctions remain detectable and feedback remains classable.

Best fit:

- O2
- O3
- F1
- F2

Typical use:

- mild environmental change,
- bounded interference,
- continuity under local variation.

---

### 11.3 R3 — Compression regime

Observation or feedback is preserved only in reduced form.

Best fit:

- O1 or O2 with downgrade posture
- F1 with weakened consequence visibility

Typical danger:

- compressed representation mistaken for full observability or full feedback strength.

---

### 11.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

Observation or feedback is tested under changed interfaces, lenses, or registration surfaces.

Best fit:

- O2 or O3 if same bounded question survives
- F1 or F2 if consequence remains classable

Typical danger:

- changed surface mistaken for stronger observability,
- boundary drift mistaken for adaptive feedback.

---

### 11.5 R5 — Rupture regime

Observation or feedback no longer supports the same bounded question honestly.

Possible outcomes:

- exposure only,
- degraded registration,
- noise,
- false signal,
- or new class required.

## 12. Boundary relation

Observability and feedback are boundary-dependent.

### 12.1 Observability depends on boundary permeability and structure

If nothing can cross or present at the relevant boundary, observability remains below threshold.

### 12.2 Feedback depends on boundary-mediated coupling

If contact does not become registered and state-consequential, it is not yet feedback in the stronger sense.

### 12.3 Boundary shifts can weaken or strengthen observability falsely

A cleaner display or louder perturbation can create the appearance of stronger observation or feedback without preserving the same bounded question or support basis.

Therefore:

- boundary type must remain explicit,
- changed boundary must not silently preserve the same observability claim,
- and feedback strength must not be inferred from surface vividness alone.

## 13. Relation to memory taxonomy

Memory asks:

**What remains preserved and reusable across time?**

This note asks:

**What becomes detectable, registered, and state-shaping during interaction?**

The relationship is therefore:

- observability provides distinctions that may later become memory-bearing,
- feedback shapes which registered distinctions matter for future continuity,
- memory may preserve past observability or feedback traces,
- but observation itself is not memory by default.

Useful relation:

- O1 often precedes M0 or M1 support
- O2 often supports M1 or M2
- O3 and F2 often contribute to M2
- F3 often supports M3 under bounded variation

## 14. Relation to structural identity law

Structural Identity Law answers:

**Is this still the same bounded object under the same question, constraints, support, and mechanized basis?**

This note answers:

**What kinds of distinctions or interactions can be detected, registered, and made state-consequential at that object’s boundary?**

The relationship is therefore:

- identity conservation can occur with weak observability,
- observability can increase without identity change,
- feedback can destabilize or preserve identity,
- and strong disturbance is not automatically identity rupture.

Observability and feedback should remain identity-relevant but not identity-equivalent.

## 15. Relation to declared vs mechanized and closure gate

A structure must not be called more observable or more feedback-capable than the actual implementation path justifies.

Therefore:

- displayed observability is not mechanized observability,
- rendered traces are not automatically registered traces,
- apparent response is not automatically state-shaping feedback,
- adaptive-looking behavior is not automatically mechanized adaptive feedback.

Where a stronger observability or feedback claim depends on a real bounded path, the claim must remain below full class strength if backend, reachability, honest rendering, insufficiency posture, or downgrade posture are missing.

Observability and feedback language must not outrun mechanization.

## 20. Observability failure modes

Observability most commonly fails through:

### 20.1 Occlusion
Relevant structure is present but cannot be detected under current boundary conditions.

### 20.2 Threshold miss
Signal is too weak or too unstable to count as bounded observation.

### 20.3 Decorative visibility
A surface looks clear to an operator, but the system has not actually registered a corresponding distinction.

### 20.4 Registration loss
Something was detected transiently but did not survive strongly enough for later use.

### 20.5 Boundary drift
Observation occurs under changed conditions while stronger continuity language is preserved improperly.

### 20.6 Compression blindness
Reduction collapses distinctions strongly enough that later observability claims mislead.

---

## 21. Feedback failure modes

Feedback most commonly fails through:

### 21.1 Unregistered disturbance
Contact occurs, but nothing is detectably or usefully registered.

### 21.2 False coupling
Interaction is treated as feedback even though the apparent relation is spurious.

### 21.3 Decorative response
The system appears responsive, but no real future-state consequence exists.

### 21.4 Over-amplification
Feedback magnifies perturbation into instability.

### 21.5 Under-response
Interaction is registered but produces no meaningful consequence despite needing one.

### 21.6 Rigid correction
Response occurs, but is too inflexible to remain lawful under bounded variation.

### 21.7 Scope inflation
Adaptive-looking behavior silently changes the bounded question or identity of function.

## 22. Lawful downgrade postures

When stronger observability or feedback cannot be preserved honestly, preferred downgrade postures include:

- `exposed_only`
- `detectable_only`
- `registered_only`
- `non_consequential`
- `weak_feedback`
- `decorative_response`
- `compressed_observability`
- `boundary_shift`
- `support_insufficient`
- `mechanization_gap`
- `noise_only`
- `review_required`

The governing rule is:

**when a structure can no longer support the stronger observability or feedback claim honestly, the system must weaken the claim rather than preserve stronger language for convenience.**

## 25. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger observability or feedback claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

relevant structure is actually available at the active boundary,
the distinction is detectably classable,
registration survives strongly enough for later accounting where required,
any claimed consequence is real rather than decorative,
the same declared constraints remain active,
and any required implementation path is actually mechanized.

If not, the structure may still be useful.
But it must not silently preserve the stronger observability or feedback class.

## 26. One-line practical rule

In DME, observability is bounded detectability that becomes registered without invented support, and feedback is registered boundary interaction that lawfully shapes future state; when either weakens, the system must downgrade the claim rather than preserve stronger language by convenience.

## 27. One-line summary

The DME observability and feedback taxonomy distinguishes exposure, detectability, registration, operative observation, and feedback consequence so interaction, perturbation, and response can be discussed with bounded continuity, explicit downgrade posture, and clear separation from memory and identity claims.

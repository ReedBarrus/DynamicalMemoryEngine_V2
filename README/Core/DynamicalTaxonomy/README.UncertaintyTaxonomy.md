## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough replay, retention, compression, reconstruction, memory, coherence, invariance, distortion, and identity pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer a recurring question:

**When is the system not justified in saying more?**

Without a bounded uncertainty taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- weak support being mistaken for valid continuity,
- near-threshold structure being rounded into stronger classification,
- missing evidence being silently replaced by coherent narrative,
- compression ambiguity being mistaken for preserved meaning,
- unresolved posture being treated as indecision rather than lawfulness,
- and confidence language outrunning what the bounded seam actually justifies.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, uncertainty is not mere ignorance or inconvenience. Uncertainty is the bounded condition in which available support, distinction, mechanism, or class evidence is insufficient to justify a stronger claim under the active bounded question.**

Corollary rules:

- uncertainty is not automatically failure,
- uncertainty is not automatically noise,
- uncertainty is not automatically distortion,
- uncertainty is not automatically low usefulness,
- and unresolved posture is not a defect when stronger confidence would be dishonest.

If a stronger claim cannot be supported strongly enough, the system must preserve explicit uncertainty rather than inflate confidence for convenience.

## 4. Definition of uncertainty

In DME, **uncertainty** means:

> the bounded condition in which the available distinctions, support, lineage, mechanized path, or class evidence are not strong enough to justify one stronger interpretation, classification, continuity claim, or action posture over another under the active bounded question.

This means uncertainty is always:

- question-relative,
- class-relative,
- support-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- boundary-relative where crossing matters,
- regime-relative,
- and mechanization-relative where a real path matters.

Uncertainty is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- something is noisy,
- something looks complex,
- something has not been named yet,
- something feels ambiguous broadly,
- or someone prefers a stronger answer than the seam can currently justify.

## 5. Uncertainty is not noise, distortion, drift, or failure

These are related, but not the same.

### Noise

Noise is variation relative to a bounded question.

Uncertainty asks whether the system can lawfully determine what that variation means.

### Distortion

Distortion is structured deviation from required invariance.

Uncertainty asks whether the system can lawfully determine whether such deviation has crossed a meaningful threshold.

### Drift

Drift is change in question, class, support, or constraint posture across time or seams.

Uncertainty asks whether that drift has become strong enough to require reclassification or downgrade.

### Failure

Failure means the stronger claim is no longer supportable.

Uncertainty means the system cannot yet justify either strong preservation or strong failure.

One-line distinction:

**Uncertainty is not what changed. It is the lawful limit on what the system can presently justify about that change.**

## 6. The five uncertainty classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes five primary uncertainty classes.

---

### 6.1 U0 — Low uncertainty

#### Definition

The bounded question remains classable with high confidence under the declared constraints and support basis.

#### Bounded question

**Is the available evidence strong enough for confident classing?**

#### Primary role

- mark stable interpretive ground,
- support strong bounded classification,
- allow tighter review and lower downgrade pressure.

#### Common examples

- same-lens replay support with clear lineage and sufficient evidence,
- bounded identity question with surviving invariants and explicit mechanized basis,
- clear class support in review or runtime accounting.

#### Main failure modes

- false certainty,
- hidden threshold pressure,
- confidence inflation from smooth surfaces.

#### One-line summary

**U0 is lawful low-ambiguity classification.**

---

### 6.2 U1 — Bounded uncertainty

#### Definition

The bounded question remains usable and partly classable, but one or more relevant thresholds remain near, weak, or not fully resolved.

#### Bounded question

**Is the current classification still usable, but not strongly closed?**

#### Primary role

- preserve explicit caution,
- support narrow use without overclaim,
- keep the seam classable without pretending full certainty.

#### Common examples

- compression outcome that still supports a narrow replay question but sits close to support threshold,
- anomaly signal that is plausible but not strongly classed,
- identity continuity that appears preserved under a narrow use but remains downgrade-sensitive.

#### Main failure modes

- ambiguity being rounded upward,
- consultation over-reading,
- minting pressure outrunning seam certainty.

#### One-line summary

**U1 is usable but threshold-sensitive classification.**

---

### 6.3 U2 — Competing-interpretation uncertainty

#### Definition

More than one bounded classification remains plausible under the current support surface, and the system cannot yet lawfully privilege one without stronger evidence.

#### Bounded question

**Are multiple class readings still live under the current evidence?**

#### Primary role

- preserve alternative lawful readings,
- prevent premature collapse into one convenient narrative,
- support split, defer, or review posture.

#### Common examples

- structure that may be anomaly or compressed support artifact,
- replay object that may preserve M1 or M2 depending on support sufficiency,
- cross-layer object that may be one continuity object or two stitched ones.

#### Main failure modes

- narrative closure by convenience,
- hidden class inflation,
- premature merge of alternatives.

#### One-line summary

**U2 is unresolved competition between live bounded readings.**

---

### 6.4 U3 — Mechanization uncertainty

#### Definition

The object may be well-described, well-rendered, or strongly declared, but the system cannot justify the stronger claim because the actual mechanized path remains absent, partial, or insufficiently exposed.

#### Bounded question

**Is the ambiguity due to missing or insufficient mechanized basis rather than only to weak structure?**

#### Primary role

- enforce anti-overclaim posture,
- separate visible shape from real capability,
- support declared/displayed/partially mechanized status honesty.

#### Common examples

- rendered review surface without full backend reachability,
- replay-like or request-like object whose mechanism remains partial,
- classification logic that is well phrased but not yet implemented at the active seam.

#### Main failure modes

- declared = mechanized inflation,
- displayed = mechanized inflation,
- fake closure by interface clarity.

#### One-line summary

**U3 is uncertainty caused by insufficient mechanized basis.**

---

### 6.5 U4 — High uncertainty / unresolved seam

#### Definition

Available support is too weak, too degraded, too crossed, or too ambiguous for confident bounded classing at the active seam.

#### Bounded question

**Has the seam fallen below lawful confident classification?**

#### Primary role

- preserve unresolved posture,
- block stronger claims,
- trigger downgrade, split, defer, or review.

#### Common examples

- cross-layer object after strong compression with degraded support,
- replay or retention surface after support collapse,
- identity question after distortion threshold pressure where strong continuity and strong break are both insufficiently supported.

#### Main failure modes

- pretending clarity,
- inventing support,
- silently choosing one interpretation to keep workflow moving.

#### One-line summary

**U4 is unresolved classification beyond honest confidence.**

## 7. Uncertainty staircase

The five classes form a practical staircase:

- **U0** = low uncertainty
- **U1** = bounded uncertainty
- **U2** = competing-interpretation uncertainty
- **U3** = mechanization uncertainty
- **U4** = high uncertainty / unresolved seam

This staircase should remain explicit.

The system must not silently round weaker certainty upward.

Examples:

- U1 is not automatically U0
- U2 is not automatically resolved by convenience
- U3 is not automatically solved by clearer UI
- U4 is not automatically failure

---

## 8. Uncertainty functions

Each uncertainty class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 8.1 Classification function

**What bounded question is trying to be answered?**

Examples:
- what class is this object?
- what continuity remains?
- what support survives?
- what kind of feedback consequence occurred?

### 8.2 Sufficiency function

**What evidence would be needed to reduce uncertainty lawfully?**

Examples:
- stronger lineage,
- more retained support,
- clearer mechanized path,
- better cross-layer alignment,
- explicit threshold declaration.

### 8.3 Posture function

**What should the system do while uncertainty remains?**

Examples:
- narrow,
- downgrade,
- defer,
- split,
- review,
- keep unresolved posture explicit.

This three-function view prevents uncertainty from collapsing into vague “unknown” language.

## 9. Uncertainty regimes

Uncertainty classes do not operate in a vacuum.

The same object may shift uncertainty class under different regimes.

The current taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 9.1 R1 — Stable regime

Evidence is steady and seam-local classing is repeatable.

Best fit:

- U0
- U1

Typical use:

- same-lens bounded review,
- strong replay support,
- stable identity classification.

---

### 9.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Small lawful variation occurs, but the same bounded question remains active.

Best fit:

- U1
- U2

Typical use:

- mild perturbation,
- bounded adaptive evaluation,
- threshold-sensitive anomaly interpretation.

---

### 9.3 R3 — Compression regime

Support or representation is reduced, increasing ambiguity around what survives.

Best fit:

- U1
- U2
- U4

Typical danger:

- compressed object still useful but not strongly classable,
- reduced detail mistaken for preserved certainty.

---

### 9.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

The active lens, retained tier, interface, or seam changes.

Best fit:

- U2
- U3
- U4

Typical danger:

- seam drift hidden by consistent-looking display,
- changed boundary mistaken for preserved same-question confidence.

---

### 9.5 R5 — Rupture regime

The system can no longer support strong bounded classification at the active seam.

Best fit:

- U4

Typical danger:

- unresolved posture being mistaken for failure avoidance rather than lawful honesty.

## 10. Uncertainty is not indecision, weakness, or lack of discipline

### 10.2 Uncertainty is not failure

It is often the honest result of bounded support insufficiency.

### 10.3 Uncertainty is not permission for semantic inflation

The fact that the seam is hard does not justify stronger claims.

### 10.4 Uncertainty is not a substitute for review

Uncertainty posture should trigger better routing, not permanent vagueness.

## 11. Uncertainty-bearing vs support-only posture

A central dynamics distinction is:

### Uncertainty-bearing

The system can explicitly name the active uncertainty class and preserve lawful posture under it.

### Support-only

A surface remains useful as trace, evidence, review aid, or continuity hint, but does not justify stronger classing.

### False closure

The system preserves stronger certainty language after the seam has already fallen below lawful confidence.

This distinction should remain explicit.

The system must not casually call something resolved when it is only:

- suggestive,
- coherent,
- useful,
- narratively convenient,
- or partially supported.

## 20. Relation to review, minting, and consultation matrix

The review matrix asks:

**What classes hold, what fails, and what should be downgraded?** :contentReference[oaicite:12]{index=12}

This note provides the uncertainty axis for that matrix.

Review should ask:

- what uncertainty class is active?
- what stronger claim is blocked by this uncertainty?
- what evidence would reduce it?
- what downgrade or defer posture is lawful while it remains?

Consultation should ask:

- is this uncertainty local, class-wide, or mechanization-driven?
- should the object be narrowed, split, or deferred?

Minting should ask:

- does the active uncertainty class block promotion?
- if yes, is narrowing enough, or must minting stop?

## 26. Uncertainty failure modes

Uncertainty most commonly fails through the following modes.

### 26.1 False closure

The system preserves stronger confidence language after the seam has already fallen below lawful support.

### 26.2 Narrative completion

A coherent story silently replaces missing support.

### 26.3 Convenience collapse

Competing live readings are flattened into one answer because workflow wants motion.

### 26.4 Mechanization concealment

Rendered clarity hides partial or absent implementation.

### 26.5 Threshold inflation

Near-threshold evidence is treated as full support.

### 26.6 Unresolved suppression

The lawful unresolved posture is treated as undesirable and replaced with stronger claims.

## 30. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger confidence claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

the active classification question is explicit,
the same bounded question remains active,
the same declared constraints remain active,
enough support survives for confident classing,
any required invariants, boundaries, memory, or identity conditions are strong enough,
and any required implementation path is actually mechanized.

If not, the seam may still be useful.

But it must not silently preserve the stronger certainty claim.

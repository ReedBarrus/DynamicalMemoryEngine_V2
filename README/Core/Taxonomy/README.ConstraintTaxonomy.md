## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough structure, replay, retention, compression, inspection, workflow, memory, observability, and boundary pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer a recurring question:

**What kind of constraint is active here, and what exactly is that constraint preserving, limiting, permitting, or breaking?**

Without a bounded constraint taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- coherence being mistaken for lawful preservation,
- similarity being mistaken for constraint satisfaction,
- visible separation being mistaken for real boundary law,
- reuse being mistaken for preserved memory,
- repeated behavior being mistaken for stable feedback,
- and continuity language outrunning what the active constraints actually support.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

---

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, a constraint is not merely a limitation. A constraint is a lawful condition on what may exist, what may change, what may cross, what may persist, what may be detected, and what may still count as the same bounded object under a declared question and regime.**

Corollary rules:

- allowed is not automatically preserved,
- coherent is not automatically lawful,
- similar is not automatically constraint-equivalent,
- visible is not automatically constrained observability,
- retained is not automatically persistence-satisfying,
- and repeated is not automatically stable adaptation.

If a structure can no longer satisfy the active constraint set strongly enough for the same bounded question, the system must not silently preserve the stronger continuity claim.

It must instead narrow, downgrade, defer, split, reclassify, or emit unresolved posture.

## 4. Definition of constraint

In DME, **constraint** means:

> a declared and/or mechanized lawful condition that governs what distinctions, transformations, crossings, continuities, or state changes remain allowed, preserved, detectable, or supportable for a bounded question under an active regime.

This means constraints are always:

- question-relative,
- support-relative,
- mechanization-relative where a real path matters,
- boundary-relative where crossing matters,
- and regime-relative under perturbation, compression, or boundary shift.

A constraint is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- something looks orderly,
- something seems useful,
- a result repeats,
- a summary feels coherent,
- a surface appears stable,
- or a boundary is rendered clearly.

## 5. Constraint is not boundary, observability, memory, or identity

Constraint, boundary, observability, memory, and identity are related, but not the same.

### Constraint

Constraint is a **lawful condition class**.

It governs:

- what is allowed,
- what is prohibited,
- what must survive,
- what must remain distinct,
- what must remain coupled,
- and what counts as lawful change.

### Boundary

Boundary is a **delimitation class**.

It governs:

- what is inside vs outside,
- what may cross,
- what may remain bounded.

### Observability

Observability is a **detectability class**.

It governs:

- what distinctions can be sensed, registered, or made consequential.

### Memory

Memory is a **continuity support class**.

It governs:

- what remains preserved and reusable across time.

### Identity

Identity is a **continuity class**.

It governs:

- what bounded structure remains the same under declared constraints.

Therefore:

- a constraint can exist without a strong observable surface,
- a boundary is one way constraints become local and legible,
- memory depends on persistence-related constraint satisfaction,
- identity depends on multiple constraint classes holding together at once,
- and feedback is one way coupling constraints become active over time.

One-line distinction:

**Constraints define lawful conditions. Boundaries localize them. Observability detects them. Memory carries what survives them. Identity is what remains the same under them.**

## 6. The seven constraint classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes seven primary constraint classes.

---

### 6.1 C0 — Formal constraints

#### Definition

Rules of valid equivalence, substitution, classification, or transformation within a declared formal system.

#### Bounded question

**What counts as lawful equivalence or valid move here?**

#### Primary role

- define exactness conditions,
- preserve valid substitution,
- govern symbolic or rule-based sameness,
- prevent category error inside formal seams.

#### Common examples

- equality or equivalence rules,
- rule-valid transformation classes,
- declared comparison criteria,
- workflow/status vocabulary law.

#### Main failure modes

- false equivalence,
- rule inconsistency,
- category error,
- invalid substitution,
- formal drift.

#### One-line summary

**C0 governs formal validity.**

---

### 6.2 C1 — Structural constraints

#### Definition

Conditions on relation, arrangement, topology, or configuration that must hold for a structure to remain the kind of structure it is.

#### Bounded question

**What relational or geometric structure must remain intact here?**

#### Primary role

- preserve shape, relation, topology, or organization,
- prevent structural collapse into mere resemblance,
- govern invariant-bearing arrangement.

#### Common examples

- transform invariant sets,
- basin or neighborhood relation structure,
- topology-preserving forms,
- internal arrangement requirements.

#### Main failure modes

- rupture,
- deformation beyond invariance,
- topology break,
- structural collapse,
- false continuity through superficial similarity.

#### One-line summary

**C1 governs structural configuration.**

---

### 6.3 C2 — Boundary constraints

#### Definition

Conditions governing separation, crossing, permeability, exclusion, and preservation at a delimitation surface.

#### Bounded question

**What may cross, what must remain separated, and what must remain bounded here?**

#### Primary role

- govern inside/outside relation,
- govern lawful crossing,
- govern exclusion, protection, and boundedness.

#### Common examples

- intake boundaries,
- observation boundaries,
- transform boundaries,
- retention boundaries,
- identity boundaries.

#### Main failure modes

- leakage,
- false separation,
- collapse of inside/outside distinction,
- occluded crossing,
- uncontrolled permeability.

#### One-line summary

**C2 governs lawful delimitation and crossing.**

---

### 6.4 C3 — Dynamical / causal constraints

#### Definition

Conditions governing lawful state transition, causal propagation, temporal order, and trajectory through state space.

#### Bounded question

**How may this structure lawfully evolve over time?**

#### Primary role

- preserve causal order,
- constrain lawful transition paths,
- govern state evolution,
- prevent impossible jumps or incoherent dynamics.

#### Common examples

- transition constraints,
- timing/order preservation,
- lawful perturbation response classes,
- trajectory evolution conditions.

#### Main failure modes

- non-causal jump,
- unstable divergence,
- temporal incoherence,
- runaway amplification,
- illegal transition class.

#### One-line summary

**C3 governs lawful evolution through time.**

---

### 6.5 C4 — Persistence constraints

#### Definition

Conditions governing what must survive across time strongly enough for later retention, reuse, replay support, or memory claims.

#### Bounded question

**What must remain preserved strongly enough for continuity to remain supportable later?**

#### Primary role

- govern survival across time,
- govern retention legitimacy,
- govern memory-class eligibility,
- constrain degradation and residue.

#### Common examples

- retained support sufficiency,
- lineage survival,
- receipt preservation,
- replay support continuity,
- memory-bearing eligibility.

#### Main failure modes

- corruption,
- detachment,
- support collapse,
- memory overclaim,
- residue mistaken for preserved continuity.

#### One-line summary

**C4 governs preserved continuity across time.**

---

### 6.6 C5 — Coupling / feedback constraints

#### Definition

Conditions governing lawful interaction, interference, sensitivity, response, and consequence between adjacent or external structures.

#### Bounded question

**How may interaction lawfully occur, and when does it become state-consequential?**

#### Primary role

- govern interaction strength,
- govern response legitimacy,
- govern feedback consequence,
- prevent decorative response or runaway coupling.

#### Common examples

- anomaly-triggering interactions,
- perturbation consequence classes,
- weighting shifts,
- feedback sensitivity postures,
- adaptive response conditions.

#### Main failure modes

- false coupling,
- unregistered disturbance,
- decorative response,
- over-amplification,
- rigid correction,
- under-response.

#### One-line summary

**C5 governs lawful interaction and consequence.**

---

### 6.7 C6 — Cross-layer consistency constraints

#### Definition

Conditions governing whether distinctions, supports, or invariants remain aligned strongly enough across multiple surfaces, lenses, or layers for the same bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**Do the relevant layers still cohere as the same bounded support structure?**

#### Primary role

- preserve alignment across layers,
- prevent layer-wise drift,
- govern multi-surface coherence,
- prevent local validity from masquerading as global continuity.

#### Common examples

- replay/support alignment,
- boundary-to-memory alignment,
- identity support across retained and active surfaces,
- cross-surface consistency between runtime, review, and retention.

#### Main failure modes

- fragmentation,
- layer misalignment,
- local coherence with global break,
- overmerge across incompatible layers,
- false whole from separately valid parts.

#### One-line summary

**C6 governs lawful cross-layer alignment.**

## 7. Constraint staircase

The seven classes form a practical staircase:

- **C0** = formal validity
- **C1** = structural configuration
- **C2** = delimitation and crossing
- **C3** = lawful evolution
- **C4** = persistence across time
- **C5** = interaction and consequence
- **C6** = cross-layer alignment

This staircase should remain explicit.

The system must not silently collapse one constraint class into another.

Examples:

- formal validity is not automatically structural preservation,
- structural preservation is not automatically persistence,
- persistence is not automatically adaptive coupling,
- local lawful response is not automatically cross-layer coherence.

## 8. Constraint functions

Each constraint class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 8.1 Allowance function

**What is permitted here?**

Examples:
- valid substitution,
- lawful crossing,
- lawful transform,
- lawful feedback response.

### 8.2 Preservation function

**What must survive here?**

Examples:
- invariants,
- structure,
- lineage,
- retained support,
- same bounded question.

### 8.3 Failure function

**What specific break occurs when this constraint no longer holds?**

Examples:
- false equivalence,
- rupture,
- leakage,
- support loss,
- runaway coupling,
- layer fragmentation.

This three-function view prevents constraint from collapsing into vague “rules” language.

## 9. Constraint regimes

Constraint classes do not operate in a vacuum.

The same constraint may succeed or fail differently depending on the active regime.

The current taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 9.1 R1 — Stable regime

Constraint conditions are steady and repeatable.

Best fit:

- C0
- C1
- C2
- C4

Typical use:

- stable replay question,
- same-lens comparison,
- steady retention,
- repeatable bounded audit.

---

### 9.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Constraint conditions vary slightly, but the same bounded question remains lawful.

Best fit:

- C1
- C2
- C3
- C5
- C6

Typical use:

- mild perturbation,
- local variation,
- small replay change,
- bounded adaptive response.

---

### 9.3 R3 — Compression regime

Constraint satisfaction weakens because representation or support is reduced.

Best fit:

- C1
- C4
- C6

Typical danger:

- compressed support mistaken for preserved full continuity,
- reduced representation mistaken for same structural question.

---

### 9.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

The active interface, lens, tier, or observational surface changes.

Best fit:

- C2
- C4
- C5
- C6

Typical danger:

- changed boundary mistaken for same support conditions,
- shifted lens mistaken for same continuity class.

---

### 9.5 R5 — Rupture regime

The active constraint set no longer preserves the same bounded question honestly.

Possible outcomes:

- degraded continuity,
- residue,
- unresolved posture,
- new identity class required,
- or broken continuity.

Typical danger:

- preserving stronger constraint language for convenience.

## 10. Constraint is not coherence alone

Several surfaces can look constraint-satisfying without actually preserving the right class of lawfulness.

### 10.1 Coherence is not automatically lawful preservation

A structure may look coherent while violating:
- the bounded question,
- the declared constraints,
- required support,
- or mechanized basis.

### 10.2 Similarity is not automatically constraint-equivalence

Two surfaces may resemble one another while differing in:
- lineage,
- lens,
- tier,
- transform invariants,
- or allowed use.

### 10.3 Repetition is not automatically adaptive stability

Something may recur repeatedly while still violating:
- persistence constraints,
- coupling legitimacy,
- or cross-layer coherence.

### 10.4 Visibility is not automatically observability constraint satisfaction

A rendered or vivid surface may look clearly present while failing:
- detectability,
- registration,
- or state consequence.

## 11. Constraint-bearing vs support-only posture

A central dynamics distinction is:

### Constraint-bearing

A surface, object, or process supports a real bounded constraint claim at its current class.

### Support-only

A surface remains useful as evidence, lineage, display, or review aid, but does not justify the stronger claim that the active constraint is actually satisfied.

### Residual order

A surface still appears ordered, but no longer preserves the lawful condition that made the original constraint class meaningful.

This distinction should remain explicit.

The system must not casually call something constraint-preserving when it is only:

- similar,
- convenient,
- narratively coherent,
- visually tidy,
- or partially supported.

## 12. Relation to boundary taxonomy

Boundary asks:

**What kind of delimitation surface is active, and what crossing or preservation does it support?**

This note asks:

**What lawful conditions govern what may cross, what must survive, and what may still count as preserved?**

The relationship is therefore:

- C2 is the direct parent class for boundary logic,
- boundaries are one localized expression of constraint,
- boundary failure is one way broader constraint failure becomes visible,
- and boundary classes inherit from but do not replace underlying constraint classes.

Useful relation:

- C2 conditions B0–B4 directly
- C4 strongly conditions B3
- C6 strongly conditions B4
- C5 often determines how boundary interaction becomes feedback-relevant

## 13. Relation to observability and feedback taxonomy

Observability asks:

**What distinctions can become detectable and registered under active conditions?**

Feedback asks:

**What interaction becomes state-consequential?**

This note asks:

**What lawful conditions make that detectability, registration, and consequence possible or impossible?**

The relationship is therefore:

- C2 and C5 are the main parent classes for observability and feedback,
- C3 constrains lawful consequence through time,
- C6 constrains whether observed or feedback-shaped change still aligns across surfaces.

Useful relation:

- C2 often conditions O0–O2
- C5 often conditions F1–F3
- C3 conditions whether response is lawful rather than decorative
- C6 conditions whether feedback consequence remains continuity-relevant

## 14. Relation to memory taxonomy

Memory asks:

**What remains preserved and reusable across time?**

This note asks:

**What lawful conditions must hold for preservation and reuse to count honestly?**

The relationship is therefore:

- C4 is the direct parent class for memory logic,
- C2 and C6 strongly condition higher memory claims,
- memory failure is often a persistence-constraint failure, not merely a storage failure.

Useful relation:

- C4 often conditions M0–M3
- C6 strongly conditions M2–M3
- C2 often conditions whether retained material crossed lawfully into later use
- C3 and C5 matter when reuse depends on adaptive or operative paths

## 15. Relation to structural identity law

Structural Identity Law answers:

**Is this still the same bounded object under the same question, constraints, support, and mechanized basis?**

This note answers:

**What kinds of lawful conditions must still hold for that question, support, and continuity to remain valid at all?**

The relationship is therefore:

- structural identity depends on multiple constraint classes holding at once,
- identity failure is often downstream of question, boundary, persistence, or cross-layer constraint failure,
- and structural identity should be treated as a constraint-integrating continuity judgment rather than one standalone rule.

This is why identity remains:
- question-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- support-relative,
- and mechanization-relative.

## 21. Constraint failure modes

Constraint most commonly fails through the following modes.

### 21.1 False validity

A surface is treated as lawful under a rule or equivalence class it does not actually satisfy.

### 21.2 Structural rupture

A required relation, topology, or invariant configuration no longer survives.

### 21.3 Boundary collapse

Inside/outside, crossing/non-crossing, or preserved/not-preserved can no longer be maintained honestly.

### 21.4 Illegal transition

The system implies a lawful causal step that the active state path does not actually support.

### 21.5 Persistence failure

Not enough support survives across time for later retention, replay, or reuse claims.

### 21.6 False coupling

Interaction is treated as meaningful feedback even though the relation is spurious, decorative, or non-consequential.

### 21.7 Cross-layer fragmentation

Local surfaces remain separately coherent, but the same bounded question can no longer be supported across them as one continuity object.

### 21.8 Constraint drift

The active rule, lens, tier, regime, or scope changed while preserving the old continuity language.

## 25. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger constraint claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

the relevant lawful condition is explicit,
the same bounded question remains active,
the same declared constraints remain active,
enough support survives for the lawful condition to remain answerable honestly,
any required preserved invariants actually survive,
and any required implementation path is actually mechanized.

If not, the surface may still be useful.

But it must not silently preserve the stronger constraint class.

## 26. One-line practical rule

In DME, a constraint is a lawful condition on what may remain valid, bounded, preserved, or consequential for a declared question under declared conditions; when that lawfulness weakens, the system must downgrade the claim rather than preserve stronger continuity language by convenience.

## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough ingestion, segmentation, transform, compression, replay, retention, inspection, and workflow pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer a recurring question:

**What kind of boundary is active here, and what does that boundary actually permit, preserve, expose, or constrain?**

Without a bounded boundary taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- exposure being mistaken for observation,
- segmentation being mistaken for identity preservation,
- transform separation being mistaken for preserved continuity,
- replay surfaces being mistaken for reconstruction authority,
- retention tiers being mistaken for durable boundary mechanics,
- and continuity language outrunning what the active boundary actually preserves or excludes.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

---

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, a boundary is not merely a line of separation. A boundary is a lawful delimitation surface that determines what may enter, what may be preserved, what may be distinguished, what may be transformed, and what may remain bounded under a declared question and constraint regime.**

Corollary rules:

- separated is not automatically bounded,
- visible is not automatically observable,
- segmented is not automatically identity-preserving,
- transformed is not automatically continuous,
- retained is not automatically durable,
- and replay-facing is not automatically reconstruction-bearing.

If a boundary no longer preserves the same bounded question honestly under the same declared constraints, the system must not silently preserve the stronger boundary claim.

It must instead narrow, downgrade, defer, split, or classify the surface more weakly.

## 4. Definition of boundary

In DME, **boundary** means:

> a declared and/or mechanized delimitation surface that determines what counts as inside, outside, crossing, preserved, observable, transformable, or reusable for a bounded question under declared constraints.

This means boundaries are always:

- question-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- support-relative,
- mechanization-relative where a real path matters,
- and regime-relative under perturbation, compression, or boundary shift.

A boundary is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- something looks separate,
- something is rendered in a different pane,
- a segment exists,
- a transform produced a new representation,
- a replay surface has a frame,
- or a stored object has a file boundary.

## 5. Boundary is not memory, observability, or identity

Boundary, memory, observability, and identity are related, but not the same.

### Boundary

Boundary is a **delimitation class**.

It governs:

- what is inside vs outside,
- what may cross,
- what may remain bounded,
- what conditions must hold for continuity.

### Memory

Memory is a **continuity support class**.

It governs:

- what remains preserved and reusable across time.

### Observability

Observability is a **detectability class**.

It governs:

- what distinctions can be sensed, registered, or made consequential.

### Identity

Identity is a **continuity class**.

It governs:

- what bounded structure remains the same under declared constraints.

Therefore:

- a boundary can exist without memory,
- memory can persist across a weakened boundary,
- observability can change because of a boundary shift without changing identity,
- and identity can fail because a critical boundary function was not preserved.

One-line distinction:

**Boundary determines lawful separation and crossing. Memory determines retained reuse. Observability determines detectable distinction. Identity determines conserved continuity.**

## 6. The five boundary classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes five primary boundary classes.

---

### 6.1 B0 — Contact boundary

#### Definition

The most minimal separation surface at which an inside/outside relation or interaction opportunity exists.

#### Bounded question

**Is there any lawful contact surface at all?**

#### Constraint relation

Separation / contact constraint only.

#### Primary role

- admits or blocks raw encounter,
- defines a minimal inside/outside relation,
- provides the precondition for observability or feedback.

#### Not enough for

- observation,
- registration,
- transform continuity,
- retention legitimacy,
- or identity preservation.

#### Common examples

- ingress seam,
- raw interface contact,
- exposure surface,
- initial operator/runtime handoff surface.

#### Main failure modes

- no real separation,
- false contact,
- decorative interface,
- contact without usable delimitation.

#### One-line summary

**B0 is a minimal contact surface.**

---

### 6.2 B1 — Observational boundary

#### Definition

A contact surface under which structure or change can be detectably exposed for a bounded observation question.

#### Bounded question

**What can be lawfully observed at this boundary?**

#### Constraint relation

Contact + detectability constraints.

#### Primary role

- frames observation,
- defines what counts as accessible signal,
- determines what distinctions can become observable.

#### Not enough for

- transform equivalence,
- durable memory,
- or identity continuity by itself.

#### Common examples

- windowed observation frame,
- replay inspection surface,
- anomaly comparison surface,
- bounded query inspection frame.

#### Main failure modes

- occlusion,
- threshold miss,
- observational distortion,
- false visibility,
- unstable observation window.

#### One-line summary

**B1 is a boundary for lawful observation.**

---

### 6.3 B2 — Transform boundary

#### Definition

A boundary across which structure is remapped into a new representational geometry while preserving only declared invariants and bounded use conditions.

#### Bounded question

**What may change representation here without silently changing the bounded object or question?**

#### Constraint relation

Observation + representation / transformation constraints.

#### Primary role

- separates input structure from transform-space structure,
- declares what invariants are meant to survive,
- exposes how remapping changes what can later be compared.

#### Not enough for

- automatic continuity,
- identity preservation,
- or operational memory by itself.

#### Common examples

- raw-to-spectral transform seam,
- feature-extraction seam,
- compression entry seam,
- lens remapping surface.

#### Main failure modes

- lens inflation,
- invariant loss,
- representation drift,
- transform equivalence overclaim,
- continuity illusion.

#### One-line summary

**B2 is a boundary across representational remapping.**

---

### 6.4 B3 — Retention boundary

#### Definition

A boundary that determines what preserved structure, lineage, support, or tier posture survives strongly enough to remain available later.

#### Bounded question

**What can lawfully cross from present structure into later retained availability?**

#### Constraint relation

Persistence + lineage + retention / tier constraints.

#### Primary role

- governs retention,
- separates live state from retained state,
- determines what survives as support, lineage, replay substrate, or memory class candidate.

#### Not enough for

- operational memory by itself,
- reconstruction authority,
- or identity conservation by itself.

#### Common examples

- receipt preservation seam,
- retained-tier ladder seam,
- digest vs receipt separation,
- archive / pin / retained support surface.

#### Main failure modes

- detachment,
- lineage loss,
- retention inflation,
- preserved residue mistaken for memory,
- replay legitimacy overclaim.

#### One-line summary

**B3 is a boundary for lawful preservation into later use.**

---

### 6.5 B4 — Identity boundary

#### Definition

A boundary that determines whether the same bounded object can still be treated as the same under the same declared question, constraints, support, and mechanized basis.

#### Bounded question

**Does this boundary still preserve the same bounded object honestly?**

#### Constraint relation

Question + declared constraint + support + mechanization + distortion threshold constraints.

#### Primary role

- governs continuity claims,
- separates same-object conservation from degraded, narrowed, or broken continuity,
- anchors later identity hierarchy application.

#### Common examples

- structural identity audit seam,
- replay continuity threshold seam,
- compression-reminting accountability seam,
- packet identity / workflow continuity seam.

#### Main failure modes

- question drift,
- constraint drift,
- support collapse,
- mechanization gap,
- distortion beyond continuity threshold,
- false sameness.

#### One-line summary

**B4 is a boundary for lawful continuity of the same bounded object.**

## 7. Boundary staircase

The five classes form a staircase:

- **B0** = contact
- **B1** = observable contact
- **B2** = transform/remap boundary
- **B3** = retention/preservation boundary
- **B4** = identity continuity boundary

This staircase should remain explicit.

A higher boundary class includes stronger claims than the class below it.

The system must not silently round weaker classes upward.

Examples:

- B0 is not automatically B1
- B1 is not automatically B2
- B2 is not automatically B3
- B3 is not automatically B4

---

## 8. Boundary functions

Each boundary class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 8.1 Separation function

**What is kept distinct here?**

Examples:
- inside vs outside,
- live vs retained,
- raw vs transformed,
- same-object vs new-object posture.

### 8.2 Crossing function

**What can lawfully cross this boundary?**

Examples:
- signal,
- lineage,
- support,
- transforms,
- perturbation,
- replay traces.

### 8.3 Preservation function

**What must remain intact for the bounded question to survive this crossing honestly?**

Examples:
- detectability,
- declared invariants,
- lineage,
- retained-tier posture,
- support sufficiency,
- mechanized basis.

This three-function view prevents boundary from collapsing into mere edge or container language.

## 9. Boundary regimes

Boundary classes do not operate in a vacuum.

The same boundary may succeed or fail differently depending on the active regime.

The current taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 9.1 R1 — Stable contact regime

Boundary conditions are steady and crossing behavior is repeatable.

Best fit:

- B0
- B1

Typical use:

- stable intake,
- steady observation window,
- repeatable exposure surface.

---

### 9.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Boundary remains lawful under small variation while preserving the same bounded question.

Best fit:

- B1
- B2
- B3

Typical use:

- mild input variation,
- modest transform variation,
- bounded replay / retention change.

---

### 9.3 R3 — Compression regime

Boundary remains partly useful after reduction, but preserved distinctions weaken.

Best fit:

- B2
- B3
- B4 with downgrade posture

Typical danger:

- compressed boundary output mistaken for preserved continuity.

---

### 9.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

The active boundary itself changes: lens, interface, tier, or observation surface shifts.

Best fit:

- all classes, with explicit downgrade posture

Typical use:

- changed windowing,
- changed transform lens,
- changed retained tier,
- changed replay or query interface.

Typical danger:

- changed boundary mistaken for same boundary class with unchanged strength.

---

### 9.5 R5 — Rupture regime

The active boundary no longer preserves the same bounded crossing or preservation question honestly.

Possible outcomes:

- contact only,
- degraded observation,
- partial transform trace,
- retained residue,
- new identity class required,
- or broken continuity.

Typical danger:

- preserving stronger boundary language for convenience.

## 10. Boundary is not segmentation, transform, or retention alone

Several surfaces can look boundary-like without qualifying for stronger boundary claims.

### 10.1 Segmentation is not automatically a preserved boundary

A window or segment may isolate a local object while still failing to preserve the same observational or identity question.

### 10.2 Transform is not automatically a continuity-preserving boundary

A remapped structure may remain useful while failing to preserve the declared invariants needed for stronger continuity claims.

### 10.3 Retention is not automatically a lawful retention boundary

Stored or archived material may remain preserved without retaining enough lineage, tier honesty, or replay legitimacy for stronger claims.

### 10.4 Rendered edges are not automatically mechanized boundaries

A visible pane, panel, or interface division is not automatically a mechanized boundary class unless a real bounded path governs what crosses and what is preserved.

## 11. Boundary-bearing vs support-only posture

A central dynamics distinction is:

### Boundary-bearing

A surface supports a real bounded boundary claim at its current class.

### Support-only

A surface remains useful as a support, display, lineage carrier, or inspection aid, but does not justify the stronger boundary claim.

### Residual separation

A surface still appears separated, but no longer preserves the crossing or continuity function that made the original boundary meaningful.

This distinction should remain explicit.

The system must not casually call something a strong boundary when it is only:

- a display edge,
- a segment handle,
- a replay frame,
- a transform output,
- or retained residue.

## 12. Relation to observability and feedback

Observability asks:

**What can become detectably available under the active boundary?**

Feedback asks:

**What crossing or interference becomes consequential for later state?**

The relationship is therefore:

- B0 often enables O0 or F0,
- B1 often enables O1–O2,
- B2 often changes what is observable or feedback-relevant,
- B3 often determines whether feedback traces or observed distinctions persist,
- B4 determines whether boundary-mediated change still preserves the same bounded object.

Boundary should therefore be treated as the lawful delimitation layer beneath observability and feedback.

---

## 13. Relation to memory taxonomy

Memory asks:

**What remains preserved and reusable across time?**

This note asks:

**What boundary determines whether crossing into later preserved use is lawful, legible, and continuity-relevant?**

The relationship is therefore:

- B3 is the most direct precondition for memory classes,
- B4 determines whether preserved structure still supports the same bounded object,
- memory can be downgraded because a boundary failed,
- and retained material can survive even when a stronger boundary class does not.

Useful relation:

- B3 often conditions M0 and M1
- B4 often conditions M2 and M3
- boundary shift often appears as memory regime shift
- retention without lawful boundary preservation often becomes residue rather than stronger memory

## 14. Relation to structural identity law

Structural Identity Law answers:

**Is this still the same bounded object under the same question, constraints, support, and mechanized basis?**

This note answers:

**What type of delimitation surface is active, and what kind of crossing, preservation, or continuity can it lawfully support?**

The relationship is therefore:

- B4 is the boundary class most directly coupled to structural identity,
- B0–B3 may remain lawful while B4 fails,
- and apparent boundary continuity must not be mistaken for conserved identity.

Boundary audit and identity audit should therefore remain related but distinct.

## 15. Relation to declared vs mechanized and closure gate

A surface must not be called a stronger boundary than the actual implementation path justifies.

Therefore:

- declared boundary is not mechanized boundary,
- displayed separation is not mechanized boundary,
- pane layout is not boundary function,
- replay frame is not reconstruction boundary by default,
- retention language is not retention mechanics by default.

Where a stronger boundary claim depends on a real bounded path, the claim must remain below full class strength if the backend, reachability, rendering honesty, insufficiency posture, or downgrade posture are missing.

Boundary language must not outrun mechanization.

## 20. Boundary failure modes

Boundary most commonly fails through the following modes.

### 20.1 False separation

A surface appears distinct, but no real bounded crossing or preservation rule exists.

### 20.2 Occluded crossing

The boundary exists, but the relevant structure cannot cross or present strongly enough for the bounded question.

### 20.3 Invariant loss

A transform or compression boundary no longer preserves the declared invariants needed for stronger continuity claims.

### 20.4 Retention inflation

A retained surface is treated as though it crossed a lawful preservation boundary more strongly than the surviving support justifies.

### 20.5 Question drift

The same boundary language is preserved while the bounded question changes.

### 20.6 Constraint drift

The active lens, tier, route, or interface changed while preserving the old boundary language.

### 20.7 Mechanization gap

A stronger boundary claim depends on a real bounded path that does not actually exist.

### 20.8 Boundary collapse

The system can no longer lawfully distinguish inside/outside, crossing/non-crossing, or preserved/not-preserved at the active seam.

When stronger boundary function cannot be preserved honestly, preferred downgrade postures include:

- `contact_only`

- `retained_only`
- `identity_not_preserved`
- `compressed_boundary`
- `boundary_shift`
- `constraint_drift`
- `question_drift`
- `mechanization_gap`
- `support_insufficient`
- `review_required`

The governing rule is:

**when a surface can no longer support the stronger boundary claim honestly, the system must weaken the claim rather than preserve stronger boundary language for convenience.**

## 22. Boundary outcomes

A practical boundary audit should produce one of the following broad outcomes.

### 22.1 Contact only

B0 holds; stronger classes do not.

### 22.2 Observationally bounded

B1 holds; stronger transform, retention, or identity claims remain below threshold.

### 22.3 Transform-bounded

B2 holds for the declared representational remap.

### 22.4 Retention-bounded

B3 holds for preserved support or lineage crossing into later use.

### 22.5 Identity-bounded

B4 holds for the same bounded object under the declared constraints.

### 22.6 Degraded boundary

Useful separation remains, but not enough to preserve the old class without explicit weakening.

### 22.7 Residual separation only

A surface still appears distinct, but no longer supports the prior boundary function honestly.

### 22.8 Unresolved

Support is too weak to justify either strong continuity or strong failure.

## 24. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger boundary claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

a real inside/outside or crossing relation exists,
the same bounded question remains explicit,
the same declared constraints remain active,
enough support survives for the same crossing or preservation question to remain answerable honestly,
any claimed preserved invariants actually survive,
and any required boundary path is actually mechanized.

If not, the surface may still be useful.

But it must not silently preserve the stronger boundary class.

## 25. One-line practical rule

In DME, a boundary is a lawful delimitation surface that governs what may cross, what may be preserved, and what may remain bounded for a declared question under declared constraints; when that function weakens, the system must downgrade the boundary claim rather than preserve stronger language by convenience.

## 26. One-line summary

The DME boundary taxonomy distinguishes contact, observational, transform, retention, and identity boundaries so separation, crossing, preservation, and continuity can be discussed with bounded honesty, explicit downgrade posture, and clear linkage to memory, observability, and identity claims.

## 1. Why this note exists

DME now has enough structure, replay, retention, observability, feedback, memory, and identity pressure that the project needs one explicit place to answer a recurring question:

**What kind of coherence is present here, and how strong is it relative to the bounded question being asked?**

Without a bounded coherence taxonomy, several distortion risks become active:

- local order being mistaken for robust continuity,
- similarity being mistaken for structural coherence,
- replay vividness being mistaken for preserved coherence,
- adaptive-looking response being mistaken for stable coherence,
- cross-surface agreement being mistaken for cross-layer coherence,
- and continuity language outrunning the actual integration quality of the structure.

This note exists to prevent those drifts.

## 2. Core rule

**In DME, coherence is not mere order, smoothness, usefulness, or resemblance. Coherence is the lawful degree to which relevant distinctions, relations, transitions, and supports remain integrated strongly enough for a bounded question to stay answerable under declared constraints.**

Corollary rules:

- orderly is not automatically coherent,
- repeated is not automatically stable,
- similar is not automatically integrated,
- vivid is not automatically support-preserving,
- adaptive-looking is not automatically lawful adaptation,
- and local agreement is not automatically whole-object coherence.

If the active coherence class weakens below what the bounded question requires, the system must not silently preserve the stronger continuity claim.

It must instead narrow, downgrade, defer, split, reclassify, or emit unresolved posture.

## 4. Definition of coherence

In DME, **coherence** means:

> the lawful degree to which a structure, relation set, transition path, support basis, or multi-surface object remains integrated strongly enough that the active bounded question can still be answered without inventing support or flattening meaningful distinctions.

This means coherence is always:

- question-relative,
- constraint-relative,
- support-relative,
- boundary-relative where crossing matters,
- regime-relative under perturbation, compression, or boundary shift,
- and mechanization-relative where a real path matters.

Coherence is **not** guaranteed merely because:

- something looks smooth,
- something repeats,
- something compresses well,
- something feels unified,
- multiple surfaces agree superficially,
- or a structure remains useful for some broader question.

## 5. Coherence is not constraint, boundary, observability, memory, or identity

Coherence depends on these, but is not reducible to any one of them.

### Constraint
Constraint governs:
- what is lawful,
- what must be preserved,
- what may count as valid.

### Boundary
Boundary governs:
- what is separated,
- what may cross,
- what may remain bounded.

### Observability / Feedback
Observability and feedback govern:
- what distinctions can be detected,
- what interactions become consequential.

### Memory
Memory governs:
- what remains preserved and reusable across time.

### Identity
Identity governs:
- what bounded object remains the same at an active class.

### Coherence
Coherence governs:
- how strongly the relevant distinctions, supports, relations, and transitions hold together for the bounded question.

Therefore:

- constraints can hold while coherence is weak,
- observability can be high while coherence is low,
- memory can remain while coherence degrades,
- identity can narrow because coherence weakened,
- and coherence can strengthen without automatically raising identity class.

One-line distinction:

**Constraints define lawfulness. Coherence measures how strongly the lawful parts still hold together for the bounded question.**

## 6. The six coherence classes

The current bounded taxonomy recognizes six primary coherence classes.

---

### 6.1 H0 — Local coherence

#### Definition

Immediate local order or integration at a single bounded seam, frame, or surface.

#### Bounded question

**Does this local structure hold together at all?**

#### Primary role

- detect minimal internal order,
- distinguish noise from bounded local organization,
- provide the lowest useful coherence class.

#### Common examples

- a local window with stable internal pattern,
- a single bounded output with non-random internal structure,
- a coherent local state without broader continuity claims.

#### Main failure modes

- noise dominance,
- fragmentary local collapse,
- decorative regularity without meaningful bounded support.

#### One-line summary

**H0 is local bounded order.**

---

### 6.2 H1 — Structural coherence

#### Definition

Integration of relevant internal relations or configuration strongly enough that the same structural question remains answerable.

#### Bounded question

**Do the relevant structural relations still hold together as one lawful configuration?**

#### Primary role

- preserve internal relation quality,
- support transform and comparison questions,
- prevent superficial resemblance from masquerading as true structure.

#### Common examples

- invariant-bearing structure under active lens,
- stable neighborhood or basin relation pattern,
- lawful shape/configuration persistence.

#### Main failure modes

- structural rupture,
- topology loss,
- relation collapse,
- false structure through summary inflation.

#### One-line summary

**H1 is lawful internal structural integration.**

---

### 6.3 H2 — Temporal coherence

#### Definition

Integration of a structure or relation set across time strongly enough that the same bounded continuity question remains answerable.

#### Bounded question

**Does this object hold together across time rather than only per-frame or per-surface?**

#### Primary role

- support continuity across runs, windows, replay, or retained surfaces,
- distinguish persistent order from momentary order.

#### Common examples

- bounded continuity across replay segments,
- stable local behavior through small temporal evolution,
- continuity that survives more than one instant.

#### Main failure modes

- drift,
- temporal collapse,
- framewise coherence without continuity,
- recurrence illusion.

#### One-line summary

**H2 is bounded continuity through time.**

---

### 6.4 H3 — Cross-boundary coherence

#### Definition

Integration that survives lawful crossing between bounded surfaces, representations, or preservation seams strongly enough to support the same bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**Does coherence survive the relevant boundary crossing honestly?**

#### Primary role

- support transform, compression, retention, and replay questions,
- distinguish boundary-preserved continuity from boundary-shaped illusion.

#### Common examples

- coherence surviving raw → transform crossing,
- coherence surviving live → retained support crossing,
- replay-facing coherence still aligned with preserved support.

#### Main failure modes

- boundary drift,
- representational inflation,
- compression blindness,
- preserved shape without preserved support.

#### One-line summary

**H3 is coherence that survives lawful boundary crossing.**

---

### 6.5 H4 — Adaptive coherence

#### Definition

Coherence that remains lawful under bounded perturbation, interaction, or local variation without silently changing the bounded question.

#### Bounded question

**Does this structure remain integrated under bounded variation while preserving the same question?**

#### Primary role

- support perturbation resilience,
- distinguish real adaptive stability from brittle or decorative stability,
- provide the main coherence class for bounded robustness.

#### Common examples

- replay or retained support surviving bounded perturbation,
- lawful continuity under narrow variation,
- feedback-shaped persistence that remains bounded.

#### Main failure modes

- brittle rupture,
- runaway amplification,
- rigid overcorrection,
- false adaptation,
- widened-question overread.

#### One-line summary

**H4 is bounded adaptive stability.**

---

### 6.6 H5 — Cross-layer coherence

#### Definition

Integration across multiple relevant surfaces, layers, or support regimes strongly enough that the same bounded question remains answerable as one object rather than as loosely related fragments.

#### Bounded question

**Do the relevant layers still cohere as one bounded support structure?**

#### Primary role

- support whole-object continuity across active surfaces,
- prevent local validity from masquerading as global continuity,
- provide the strongest coherence class in the current taxonomy.

#### Common examples

- runtime, retained, replay, and review surfaces staying aligned for the same question,
- multi-surface support that still behaves as one bounded continuity object,
- identity-relevant coherence across more than one class of surface.

#### Main failure modes

- cross-layer fragmentation,
- local coherence with global break,
- overmerge,
- stitched-story inflation,
- false whole from separately valid parts.

#### One-line summary

**H5 is lawful integration across relevant layers.**

## 7. Coherence staircase

The six classes form a practical staircase:

- **H0** = local coherence
- **H1** = structural coherence
- **H2** = temporal coherence
- **H3** = cross-boundary coherence
- **H4** = adaptive coherence
- **H5** = cross-layer coherence

This staircase should remain explicit.

The system must not silently round weaker coherence classes upward.

Examples:

- H0 is not automatically H1
- H1 is not automatically H2
- H2 is not automatically H3
- H3 is not automatically H4
- H4 is not automatically H5

## 8. Coherence functions

Each coherence class can be analyzed through three functions.

### 8.1 Integration function

**What is being held together here?**

Examples:
- local order,
- relation sets,
- temporal continuity,
- support across boundary crossings,
- adaptive response quality,
- multi-surface alignment.

### 8.2 Preservation function

**What must remain integrated for the bounded question to stay answerable?**

Examples:
- invariants,
- relation quality,
- temporal continuity,
- retained support linkage,
- lawful adaptation,
- cross-layer alignment.

### 8.3 Failure function

**What kind of break occurs when that integration weakens?**

Examples:
- noise,
- rupture,
- drift,
- boundary-induced distortion,
- brittle failure,
- fragmentation.

This three-function view prevents coherence from collapsing into vague “goodness” language.

## 9. Coherence regimes

Coherence classes do not operate in a vacuum.

The same structure may exhibit different coherence classes under different regimes.

The current taxonomy recognizes five practical regimes.

---

### 9.1 R1 — Stable regime

Conditions are steady and the same bounded question is repeatedly answerable.

Best fit:

- H0
- H1
- H2

Typical use:

- stable observation,
- same-lens comparison,
- repeated bounded replay support.

---

### 9.2 R2 — Narrow perturbation regime

Small lawful variation occurs, but bounded continuity remains.

Best fit:

- H2
- H3
- H4

Typical use:

- mild perturbation,
- narrow replay variation,
- bounded adaptive stability.

---

### 9.3 R3 — Compression regime

Support or representation is reduced, placing integration under pressure.

Best fit:

- H1
- H3
- H5 with downgrade posture

Typical danger:

- compressed order mistaken for preserved coherence,
- flattened summary mistaken for integrated support.

---

### 9.4 R4 — Boundary-shift regime

The active boundary, lens, or retained tier changes.

Best fit:

- H3
- H4
- H5

Typical danger:

- changed surface mistaken for preserved coherence at the same class.

---

### 9.5 R5 — Rupture regime

The active coherence class no longer supports the same bounded question honestly.

Possible outcomes:

- degraded coherence,
- support-only continuity,
- fragmentary local order,
- unresolved posture,
- new identity class required,
- or broken continuity.

Typical danger:

- preserving stronger coherence language for convenience.

---

## 10. Coherence is not smoothness, usefulness, or similarity alone

Several surfaces can look coherent without actually preserving the relevant bounded class.

### 10.1 Smoothness is not automatically coherence

A surface may appear smooth while hiding:
- support collapse,
- distortion,
- or invalid continuity.

### 10.2 Usefulness is not automatically coherence

A surface may remain useful for a broader or different question while failing the original bounded integration requirement.

### 10.3 Similarity is not automatically coherence

Resemblance may preserve:
- local pattern likeness,
- broad shape,
- repeated utility,

without preserving:
- structure,
- continuity,
- support,
- or classed integration.

### 10.4 Agreement is not automatically cross-layer coherence

Multiple surfaces may say compatible-looking things while failing to preserve the same bounded support object across them.

---

## 11. Coherence-bearing vs support-only posture

A central dynamics distinction is:

### Coherence-bearing

A structure supports a real bounded coherence claim at its current class.

### Support-only

A structure remains useful as support, trace, memory, display, or local pattern evidence, but does not justify the stronger coherence claim.

### Residual order

A structure still appears ordered, but no longer preserves the bounded integration quality that made the original coherence class meaningful.

This distinction should remain explicit.

The system must not casually call something coherent when it is only:

- smooth,
- similar,
- useful,
- narratively tidy,
- replay-shaped,
- or partially aligned.

## 12. Relation to constraint taxonomy

Constraint asks:

**What lawful conditions must hold?**

This note asks:

**How strongly do the relevant lawful parts still hold together for the bounded question?**

The relationship is therefore:

- coherence is downstream of constraint satisfaction,
- different coherence classes are dominated by different constraint bundles,
- and coherence often degrades before stronger identity failure is formally declared.

Useful relation:

- H1 depends strongly on C1
- H2 depends strongly on C3 and C4
- H3 depends strongly on C2, C4, and C6
- H4 depends strongly on C3 and C5
- H5 depends strongly on C6

## 13. Relation to boundary taxonomy

Boundary asks:

**What delimitation surface is active, and what can cross it?**

This note asks:

**How much integration survives across or within that surface?**

The relationship is therefore:

- H3 is the coherence class most directly coupled to boundary crossing,
- B3 and B4 are especially important for stronger coherence classes,
- and boundary preservation is one necessary but not sufficient condition for coherence.

Useful relation:

- B1 supports H0–H1 locally
- B2 strongly conditions H3
- B3 strongly conditions H3 and H5
- B4 often determines whether higher coherence remains continuity-relevant

## 14. Relation to observability and feedback taxonomy

Observability and feedback ask:

**What distinctions can be detected, registered, and made state-consequential?**

This note asks:

**How integrated are those distinctions and consequences relative to the bounded question?**

The relationship is therefore:

- observability makes coherence legible,
- feedback can preserve or degrade coherence,
- high observability does not automatically imply high coherence,
- and adaptive feedback does not automatically imply adaptive coherence.

Useful relation:

- O1–O2 often surface H0–H2
- O3 and F2 often matter for H4
- F3 is often necessary, but not sufficient, for H4
- H5 requires cross-surface observability and feedback alignment without being reducible to either

## 15. Relation to memory taxonomy

Memory asks:

**What remains preserved and reusable across time?**

This note asks:

**How strongly does that preserved material still hold together as one bounded support object?**

The relationship is therefore:

- memory can remain while coherence degrades,
- coherence can remain locally while higher memory classes fail,
- stronger memory classes often depend on stronger coherence classes,
- and adaptive memory especially benefits from adaptive coherence.

Useful relation:

- M0 may exist with only H0 or H1
- M1 often depends on H2 and H3
- M2 often depends on H3
- M3 often depends on H4 and H5

## 16. Relation to identity hierarchy

Identity asks:

**What class of bounded object remains the same?**

This note asks:

**How strongly do the relevant supports, relations, transitions, and layers still hold together for that claim?**

The relationship is therefore:

- coherence is identity-relevant but not identity-equivalent,
- stronger identity classes require richer and more coupled coherence classes,
- and coherence degradation is often an early warning before identity downgrade.

Useful relation:

- I1 depends strongly on H1
- I2 depends strongly on H1–H5, especially H2–H5

## 18. Relation to declared vs mechanized and closure gate

Coherence language must not outrun mechanization.

## 22. Coherence failure modes

Coherence most commonly fails through the following modes.

### 22.1 Noise dominance

Local order weakens below useful bounded structure.

### 22.2 Structural disintegration

Relevant internal relations no longer hold together.

### 22.3 Temporal drift

Continuity weakens across time despite local order.

### 22.4 Boundary decoherence

Integration does not survive lawful crossing between surfaces.

### 22.5 Brittle adaptation

Apparent stability breaks under narrow perturbation.

### 22.6 Cross-layer fragmentation

Separately coherent surfaces no longer support one bounded whole.

### 22.7 False coherence

Smooth, similar, or vivid surfaces are mistaken for support-preserving integration.

### 22.8 Coherence drift

A different kind or class of coherence is present than the one the bounded question actually requires.

## 26. Decision rule

Preserve a stronger coherence claim only when all of the following are true strongly enough for the active bounded question:

the required integration class is explicit,
the same bounded question remains active,
the same declared constraints remain active,
enough support survives for that integration class to remain answerable honestly,
any required preserved relations, transitions, or cross-surface alignments actually survive,
and any required implementation path is actually mechanized.

If not, the surface may still be useful.

But it must not silently preserve the stronger coherence class.
